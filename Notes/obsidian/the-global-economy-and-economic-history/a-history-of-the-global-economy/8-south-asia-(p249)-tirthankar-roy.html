8 South Asia (P249) Tirthankar Roy

Economic History of South Asia
...

Geographic and Social Diversity
...

  • South Asia: India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Nepal, Sri Lanka, smaller nations.
  • Geographical features:
    • Himalayan mountains in the north.
    • Fertile floodplains of the Indus and the Ganges.
    • World's largest delta.
    • Long coastline, arid uplands, tropical dry forests, savannah, Thar desert.
  • Social diversity:
    • Many language groups, almost all religions.
    • Vastly different social practices across regions.
  • Political diversity:
    • Various large empires formed and disintegrated pre-Christian Era.
    • Land tax-based economies in densely populated areas.
    • Smaller, trade-dependent states in seaboard and arid zones.
    • Mughal Empire's loose integration (1526–1707), followed by semi-independent successor states.

British Colonial Impact
...

  • Political centralization by British Empire (mid-18th to mid-19th century).
  • New taxation systems and state-elite relationships.
  • Current political boundaries and federal setup challenges trace back to colonial forced union.

Economic Integration and Paradoxes
...

  • Prehistory as trading zone, colonial status led to deep economic integration.
  • Industrialization: Employment in factories grew from 100,000 (1860) to 2 million (1940).
    • Links with Britain boosted factory industry rise.
  • Loss of craft heritage.
  • Agriculture remained with low income, subsistence wages, poor yields despite export crop growth.

Debates and Research Themes
...

  • Modernization amid persistent poverty sparks economic history debates.
  • Focus on quantitative data in scholarship.
  • Divergence debate of the 2000s:
    • Shift from Europe to Asia, Africa, Latin America in inequality discussions.
    • New estimates of living standards in India.
    • Integration of South Asian history with world history.

Chapter Structure
...

  • Historiographical Debates: Precolonial and colonial South Asia.
  • Impact of the Empire: Literature review.
  • Living Standards Research: Modern inequality debates.
  • Postcolonial India: Survey and interpretation of continuity and change.

Power and Control in Precolonial South Asia(P 250)
...

Strategic Importance of Land
...

  • Empires formed at Indus and Ganges floodplains intersection.
  • Crossroads to Central Asia, Afghanistan, Iran.
  • Proximity to fertile Ganges agricultural tracts.
  • Access to northern deltas and Indian Ocean via rivers.
  • Large towns and capitals formed in these zones.

Property Rights and Military Privilege
...

  • Combination maintained power of empires.
  • Decentralized tax collection and military service system.
  • Vulnerability to local rebellions.
  • Varied impacts on merchants and artisans.

Commercial World of the Seaboard
...

  • Indian and Arab merchants, later Europeans (c. 1600).
  • Trade in textiles, spices, horses, silks.
  • Unsettled relationship between maritime and land-based worlds.

British East India Company and Colonial Transformation
...

Transition of Power
...

  • Bengal power transfer to British East India Company (1757–65).
  • Company acquired western, southern, northern territories (1765–1818).
  • Territories passed to British Crown in 1858.

Rise of the British Empire
...

  • Company: merchant firm, not land-based.
  • Success from standing army, marginalization of feudal support.
  • Factors: foreign and commercial origin, internal organization.

Economic Changes under British Rule
...

Trade and Industry Transformation
...

  • Shift: artisanal textiles exporter to agricultural commodities exporter.
  • Importer of British textiles; railways, steamships, telegraph aided this.
  • New World plantations imported South Asian labour post-slavery abolition.

Capitalist Migration and Market Guardianship
...

  • Indian capitalists moved to port cities: Bombay, Calcutta, Madras.
  • British Empire as market guardian: military power, business laws, monetary management.
  • Industrialization opportunities for Indian merchants.

Infrastructure and Welfare Limitations
...

  • Limited Empire contributions: peace, irrigation canals, railways.
  • Minimal transformation of remote rural lives.
  • Late interwar period: political blunders, nationalist backlash, mass movement.

Legacy and Debates of British Colonial Rule
...

Growth and Inequality
...

  • Debates on economic growth and inequality under British rule.
  • Nationalist view: little growth, rising inequality.
  • Early nationalists: unequal exchange with Britain, services account deficit, investment compromise.

Marxist Historians' View
...

  • Nineteenth-century globalization: specialization pattern, deindustrialization.
  • Peasant impoverishment: high taxes, rural debts, property transfer.
  • Paradigm of underdevelopment due to colonial policy.

Recent Research Trends
...

  • Main research tests underdevelopment paradigm.
  • New themes explored in the last fifteen years.

(Citations: Moreland, W., Kosambi, D. D., Sharma, R. S., Habib, I.)

The colonial Economy (P 254)
...

Early Statistical Work
...

  • Tested production, income, prices, external accounts, labour force, public saving and investment.
    • References: Sivasubramonian (2000), Heston (1983), Guha (1992), Banerji (1962), Thorner (1962), Bagchi (1976), Thavaraj (1962).
  • GDP data discussed later.
  • Balance of payments reconstruction estimated service outflow.
  • Labour force data (1872-1931) showed a decline in artisan employment.

Controversies and Inconsistencies
...

  • Immiseration model evidence inconclusive and controversial.
    • Unexplained gender differences in manufacturing participation.
    • Women's participation declined more than men's.
  • Nineteenth-century outflow included useful services from Britain, contributing to domestic income.
  • Deindustrialization slowed or stopped in the twentieth century.
  • Increased rural credit transactions; minimal land ownership transfer.
  • Studies questioned distress sale of crops, showing responsive supply to prices (Narain 1965).

Recent Research Themes
...

  • More dispassionate approach compared to nationalist and Marxist views.
  • Clingingsmith and Williamson (2008): Industrial decline traced to domestic issues in the eighteenth century.
  • Roy (1999): Revival of artisanal production and consumption after initial shock.

Testing the Hypothesis of Empire-Induced Impoverishment
...

  • Labour force and balance of payments provide indirect tests.
  • GDP trends expected to offer a more direct test.
  • Examination of GDP data needed to assess the impact.

National Income
...

Overview of National Income Estimates
...

  • Estimates consistently available from 1900 onward.
  • Uneven data between 1860 and 1900.

Key Propositions on Economic Change (1857-1947)
...

  • GDP Growth Rates
    • Modest growth (1860-1914).
    • Near-zero growth (1914-1947).
    • Source: Table 8.1.
  • Agricultural Income
    • Strongest effect on GDP.
    • Growth by expanding land frontier (1860-1914).
    • Post-1914: Land scarcity and declining fertility in older tracts.
    • Source: Table 8.1.
  • Industrial Income
    • Positive effect on GDP.
    • Combination of:
      • Robust growth in modern factories.
      • Slow growth in artisanal industry.
      • Transition from household-based to wage-based production.
    • Source: Table 8.1.
  • Services Income
    • Positive effect on GDP.
    • Structural change:
      • Decline in traditional non-wage services (e.g., bonded labour).
      • Expansion of modern wage-based services (e.g., banking, insurance, foreign trade).
    • Source: Table 8.1.
  • Labour Productivity Growth
    • Small overall growth, especially in agriculture.
    • Fastest growth in industry during the late nineteenth century.
    • Slowest growth in services despite transport modernization.
    • First half of the twentieth century:
      • Respectable growth in industry and services.
      • Stagnation in agriculture hindered overall productivity growth.
    • Source: Broadberry and Gupta (2014), Table 8.2.

Inequality in Colonial India (P 256)
...

Overview
...

  • 1891 Census: India's population too diverse for uniform statistical treatment (Baines, 1893).
  • Inequality measures describe development and test growth/stagnation models.

Patterns of Inequality
...

  • Colonial Inequality Trends
    • Rising inequality not due to the immiseration model.
    • Minimal evidence of land transfer/class polarization in rural areas.
    • Globalization favored resource-rich tracts and port cities.
    • Industrial and service wages grew faster than agricultural wages.
    • Urban-rural wage gap indicated increasing inequality.
  • Within Agriculture
    • Limited land transfer; stable land-holding inequality (Kumar, 1975).
    • Peasants resisted parting with land.
    • Wage depression due to elastic labor supply; slow wage convergence (Collins, 1999).
    • Mild inequality rise shown by declining wage-GDP ratio in agriculture (Roy, 2007).

Quantitative Analysis
...

  • Gini Coefficient Estimates
    • Atkinson's income data (1875, 1895) allows Gini calculation assuming equal peasant incomes (Atkinson, 1902).
    • 1895 Gini: 0.26-0.32, lower than Kuznets' 1950s estimates (0.4-0.5) (Kuznets, 1963).
    • Revised estimates: 0.3-0.4 (Ojha & Bhatt, 1964).
    • Slight increase in inequality plausible.
  • Income Distribution Changes
    • Urban-rural wage divergence.
    • Narrowing income gap between top and lowest earners (1875-1895).
    • Rise of a middle class: merchants, contractors, professionals, middle-to-rich peasants.

Regional Inequality and Globalization
...

  • Hypothesis
    • Globalization-induced inequality should increase regional disparity.
    • India exported agricultural commodities and resource-intensive manufactures.
    • Recent Studies
      • Provincial income observations: mixed results.
      • District data survey: supports rising inequality (1850-1900) (Caruana-Galizia, 2013; Roy, 2014).
      • Different methodologies; results not necessarily contradictory.

Paradigm Shifts
...

  • Marxist paradigm still holds local explanatory power.
  • Current research explores colonial economy beyond drain, deindustrialization, and rural credit.
  • New themes: labor productivity and public goods.

Other Themes in Colonial India's Economic History(P 258)
...

Productivity Trends
...

  • Focus on Labor Quality and Productivity
    • Recent research led by Susan Wolcott.
    • Emphasis on efficiency and work ethic in comparative growth.
    • Indian cotton mill workers exhibited low effort cultural norms.
      • High worker-to-machine ratios in textiles (Wolcott & Clark, 1999; Wolcott, 1994).
      • Alternative view: profit-maximizing response to low labor costs (Gupta, 2008).
    • Low labor cost linked to low agricultural yield.

Public Goods and Welfare
...

  • Investment and Infrastructure
    • British Indian state made limited infrastructure and welfare investments.
    • Regional variability in public goods and infrastructure.
      • High railway density near wheat production centers in Punjab and along the Ganges (Map 8.2).
    • Recent research emphasizes institutions, local autonomy, and fiscal resources (Banerjee & Iyer, 2005; Iyer, 2010; Chaudhary, 2010).
  • Railways
    • Major public-private investment in British India.
    • Fresh research views state investment favorably.

Market Integration
...

  • Efficiency and Tradability
    • Explored in studies on agricultural labor and grain markets (Collins, 1999; Studer, 2008).
    • Market integration found to be relatively small in aggregate.

Living Standards in the Long Run (P 260)
...

Divergence Debate and Historical Context (Pomeranz, 2000; Parthasarathi, 1998)
...
  • Significance: Examines when India lagged behind western Europe.
    • Requires Deep Historical Analysis: Beyond empire-focused South Asian economic history.
  • Pomeranz Contribution: 1800 China resembled western Europe (Pomeranz, 2000).
  • Parthasarathi Findings: Indian data supports early divergence (Parthasarathi, 1998).
Clusters in Measurement (Pomeranz, 2000; Parthasarathi, 1998)
...
  • Real Wage, Consumption, Morbidity-Mortality.
    • Encouragement for Research: Use of anthropometric data drives long-term living standards study.
  • Height Cluster Omitted (Baten and Inwood): Detailed discussion elsewhere in the volume.

Wage in Colonial Context
...

Traditional Usage and Limitations (Narain, 1929; Mukherjee, 1967)
...
  • Early Use by Indian Scholars: Utilized wage series, yet faced sampling bias issues.
    • Questionable Value: Due to unresolved sampling bias issues.
  • Narain and Mukherjee: Derived wage series from English and Dutch East India Company records.
    • Circa 1600 Starting Point: Relied on records for wage data.
    • Questionable Conclusions: Based on wages from foreign enclaves, not representative of general patterns.
Regional Variation and Market Dynamics (Parthasarathi, 1998)
...
  • Coastal Enclaves vs. Interior: Disparity in wages due to market dynamics.
    • Madras Example: Significant wage differences between coastal and interior areas.
  • Agricultural Wage Variation: Reflects differences in productivity and market integration.
    • Fundamental Market Condition: Large variation in wages between regions must be considered.
Long-Term Trends and Interpretations (Allen, 2005; Chandra, 1983)
...
  • Modest Decline in Real Wages: Confirmed by subsequent analyses, but extent debatable.
    • Global Trend: Consistent with trends outside England and Holland.
  • Agricultural Wage Density: Improves around 1800, aiding in testing long-term trends.
    • Munro's Survey (1796): Provides insight into living conditions and wages in South India.
Wage Surveys and Entitlements (Roy, 2011)
...
  • Systematic Surveys: Conducted by Company employees in various regions.
    • Bihar Survey (1809-10): Indicates wage levels consistent with other regions.
  • Long-Term Purchasing Power: Agricultural laborer wages remained stable until the Green Revolution.
    • Caloric Adequacy: Barely adequate for a family of four, with precarious stability.

Consumption Trends(P 263)
...

Historical Context and Nutritional Challenges (Thomas & Ramakrishnan, 1938; Aykroyd, 1940)
...
  • Limited Historical Data: Large-scale surveys on family budgets not available until the interwar years.
    • Nutrition Norms: Experts set carbohydrate consumption norms for Indians, revealing dietary challenges.
    • Dominance of Cereals: Even with purchasing power, Indian diet primarily consisted of cereals.
Dietary Challenges and Regional Variation (Indian Government, 1927)
...
  • Under-nourishment in Bihar: Agricultural laborers and their children lacked proper nutrition.
    • South India: Excessive rice consumption led to health issues like scurvy and rickets.
  • Grain Dominance: Majority of calorie intake from grains, with low protein and vegetable intake.
    • Regional Differences: Varied dietary habits but cereal preference and low protein intake were common.
Changing Consumption Patterns (Mukherjee, 1936–37)
...
  • 1930s Shifts: Diversification in cereal preference observed, along with increased consumption of certain items.
    • Cultural and Economic Factors: Relative price shifts and ecological balance influenced dietary changes.
Persistence of Nutritional Challenges (Hopper, 1999)
...
  • Postcolonial Public Health: Dietary culture persisted despite poverty and nutritional challenges.
    • Limited Improvement: Energy and protein supplies increased marginally over nearly six decades.
Non-food Consumption Dynamics (Roy, 2012c; Kumar, 1987)
...
  • Clothing and Iron Manufactures: Increased consumption of cotton cloth and iron articles.
    • Diverse Household Articles: Consumption of services and household items became more diverse.

Health Conditions in Colonial Calcutta (P 265)
...

Historical Context (Finch, 1850)
...
  • Late Eighteenth Century Custom: European residents congratulated for surviving another year.
    • Decline by 1835: Custom ceased, but health conditions remained poor.
Disease and Mortality Rates (Finch, 1850)
...
  • Survey Around 1836: Revealed high death and infant mortality rates in Calcutta.
    • Average Death Rate: 51 per 1,000; Infant Mortality Rate: 240.
  • Comparison with Later Data (Unknown): Death rate decline possibly reflects adult survival improvement.
    • Persistent Health Variability: Disparities among different communities persisted.
Environmental and Cultural Factors (Chandrasekhar, 1959)
...
  • Limited Response to Intervention: Health conditions remained poor until public health expansion in 1921.
    • High Infant Mortality: Comparable to countries like Chile and Egypt in 1901.
  • Factors Influencing Mortality: Famines, industrialization, and migration increased risks.
    • Impact of Colonies and Settlements: Congestion in labor settlements raised infant mortality.
Colonial Era Patterns
...
  • Lives of Uncertainty: Poor, unchanging conditions contrasted with dynamic commercial cities.
  • Postcolonial Transition: Did health conditions change significantly after 1947?
    • Bridging the Gap: Did postcolonial India improve health conditions and bridge societal disparities?

Postcolonial Economic Transition (P 266)
...

Continuity and Transition
...

  • Limited Historiographical Continuity: Postcolonial patterns often disconnected from colonial or precolonial eras.
    • Data Discontinuity: Break in time series around 1947 hampers comparability.

Institutional Transition
...

  • Partition and National Economy Formation: Smooth transition despite geopolitical changes.
    • Continuity in Institutions: Indigenization of bureaucracy, legislative reforms from Empire era.

Economic Policies
...

  • Import-Substitution and State-Directed Industrialization: Shift from Empire's market system.
    • Nationalist Support: Critiques of 'drain', socialist ideologies influenced policy decisions.
    • Protectionist Measures: High tariffs, non-tariff barriers, discouragement of commodity export.

Macroeconomic Shifts
...

  • State Dominance and Market Regulation: Replaced Empire's free market with public control.
    • 1980s Transition: Reduction of state dominance, opening to foreign investment and trade.

Economic Performance
...

  • Enlargement of State and GDP Growth: Significant GDP growth but limited productivity improvement.
    • Sectoral Growth: Acceleration in agriculture, manufacturing, and services.
    • Productivity Patterns: Slow agricultural productivity growth similar to colonial era.

Comparison with Colonial Era (Broadberry & Gupta, 2014)
...

  • GDP Growth Acceleration: Postcolonial rates surpass colonial era.
  • Sectoral Shifts: Green revolution transforms agriculture, state-led industrialization.
  • Productivity Parallels: Slow agricultural productivity growth persists despite overall economic growth.

P 268
...

Economic Growth Sources
...

  • Colonial Era: Private capital-driven growth with minimal state intervention.
    • Strength: Capitalistic system fostering private accumulation.
  • Postcolonial Era: State-funded industrialization and green revolution, reliant on taxpayers and foreign aid.
    • Costly Growth: Achieved at the expense of public goods, involving subsidies and neglect of healthcare and education.

State Intervention and Policy
...

  • 1950–85 Policies: Larger state, redistribution of taxes, restraint on private investment, retreat from global trade.
    • Post-1985 Changes: Revival of private investment and foreign trade, increased subsidies to farmers.
    • Mixed Outcomes: Partial withdrawal from infrastructure leading to agricultural stagnation.

Agricultural Productivity Constraint
...

  • Key Constraint: Long-term development hindered by agricultural productivity.
    • Persisting Issue: Slow agricultural growth despite overall economic progress.

Conclusion
...

  • Balanced Assessment: GDP growth in postcolonial era came at the cost of public goods and relied heavily on state funding.
  • Long-term Constraint: Agricultural productivity remains a significant challenge for Indian development.

Concluding Remarks (P 270)
...

  • Evolution of Economic History: From political discourse to diverse research on resources, institutions, and welfare.

    • Empirical Shift: Away from political agendas towards efficiency and choice.

  • Colonial Legacy: Not solely a dark age; saw urban wage rise, consumption diversification, and industrialization.

    • Challenges: Stagnant rural wage, GDP, and food consumption amid demographic transition.

  • Postcolonial Realities: Rapid GDP growth but productivity, efficiency, and nutrition stagnated.

    • Funding Sources: Reliance on foreign aid and taxpayers' money rendered growth unsustainable.

  • Liberal Reforms of 1990s: Highlighted unsustainability of previous growth model.

  • Balance Needed: Neither colonial era nor postcolonial India represented extreme darkness or a new dawn.

  • Long-term Perspective: Agricultural productivity remains a significant challenge for Indian development.
