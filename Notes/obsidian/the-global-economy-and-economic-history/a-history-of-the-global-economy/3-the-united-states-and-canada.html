3 The United States and Canada

Economic Success of the United States and Canada (p 83)
...

Richness Over Centuries
...

  • The US and Canada have consistently been among the world's richest nations for 250 years (see Figure 3.1).
  • Abundance of fertile soil and natural resources contributed to their economic prosperity.

Historical Foundations
...

  • Both nations, originally lightly populated, attracted migrants seeking access to land after 1500.
  • Governments provided extensive political and economic freedoms, strong property rights, and unbiased rule of law.

Advantageous Conditions
...

  • Governments fostered broad-based educational systems, expanding from elementary to high school and later universities.
  • Limited destructiveness of warfare on home territories allowed sustained economic progress.

Innovations and Growth
...

  • Political and economic conditions facilitated a wide range of innovations in goods, services, and economic organization.
  • Despite occasional economic downturns, the US and Canada maintained growth patterns, emerging as global economic leaders.

Pre-Colonial Development in North America (P 84)
...

Early Migration and Settlement
...

  • Early North American populations likely migrated across a land bridge near Alaska thousands of years ago.
  • Lived in small, localized groups of hunters and gatherers with lifestyles adapted to their environments.

Diverse Native American Lifestyles
...

  • Native Americans in the eastern US and Canada engaged in trapping, maize cultivation, and other crop production.
  • North-western tribes near rivers had stable food sources, while Plains Indians roamed vast territories for hunting.

Nutrition and Health
...

  • Many tribes had diets rich in calories from meat and other sources.
  • Limited impact of diseases led to favorable nutritional outcomes; Plains Indians in the 19th century were among the world's tallest populations.

European Colonialization in North America (P 85)
...

Early Norse Contact (10th Century)
...

  • Norsemen explored Canada's northeast but faced challenges in sustaining settlements.

Major European Influences (16th Century Onwards)
...

  • Spaniards, starting with Cortez in 1513, explored Mexico, Florida, and the American Southwest.
  • English, French, and Dutch settlers established colonies, encountering diverse natural resources.

Settlement Patterns and Conflicts
...

  • European settlers encroached on Native American lands, leading to conflicts and westward movements.
  • Lack of respect for Native American resource claims exacerbated tensions and conflicts.

Colonial Economic Foundations
...

  • Staple goods, like tobacco in Virginia and rice in South Carolina, drove colonial expansion.
  • Canada supplied fur, timber, and fish, while New England contributed tall trees for shipbuilding.

Market Integration and Price Fluctuations (1700s)
...

  • Pennsylvania and European ports experienced market integration with intermittent disruptions during wars (source: Dobado-Gonzalez et al., 2012).
    Figure P 86

Political Economy and Regional Differences in Early 1800s (P 86)
...

Influence of Staple Production
...

  • Staple production shaped political economy and regional differences in the early 1800s.
  • Slavery dominated southern agriculture, concentrating wealth among large plantation owners (source: North, 1966).

Southern Plantation Economy (1780-1860)
...

  • Southern plantation owners, enriched by slave labor, held significant wealth globally.
  • Education for plantation owners' children was prioritized, hindering widespread public education.

Impact of Slavery on Income Distribution
...

  • Slavery led to a skewed income distribution with wealthy plantation owners and a large slave population.
  • Small farmers formed a middle class, but education disparities persisted, especially for slaves.

Decline of Slaveholding in Northern Colonies
...

  • Gains from slaveholding diminished in the northern and middle colonies over time.
  • Governments in these regions eventually banned slavery in the late 1700s.

Northern Agriculture and Education Policies
...

  • Northern agriculture, focused on wheat and food production, led to a more equal income distribution.
  • Northern states prioritized education, offering private elementary schools and public education after 1800.

Creating an independent nation with a broad range of economic freedoms (P 87)
...

Post-French and Indian War
...

  • After the French and Indian War (1763), English hegemony extended along the eastern seaboard.
  • Tightened English regulations and increased taxes sparked colonial protests and trade embargoes (source: Unspecified).

Path to Revolution (1763-1776)
...

  • Tensions escalated with each round of regulations; the revolution erupted after the Lexington-Concord incident.
  • Continental Congress declared independence in 1776, asserting inalienable rights to life, liberty, and happiness.

American Independence (1776-1781)
...

  • American colonists, despite opposition, outlasted the British, gaining independence by 1781.

Constitutional Convention (1787)
...

  • Dissatisfaction with post-war confederation led to the 1787 Constitutional Convention.
  • American Constitution, coupled with the Bill of Rights, established unprecedented individual rights and freedoms.

Features of the Constitution
...

  • Protection of private property, patents, copyrights, free speech, religion, and trial by jury.
  • National government rights for taxation, foreign policy, and interstate trade regulation.

Enlightenment Influence and Implementation
...

  • Constitution reflected Enlightenment ideas, reaching full fruition in North America.
  • The commitment of the people and governments to follow the document was crucial.

Leadership and Peaceful Transitions
...

  • President George Washington set a precedent for peaceful transitions, not seeking a third term.

Evolution of Democracy and Economic Freedoms (P 88)
...

Chief Justice John Marshall's Legacy
...

  • Under Chief Justice John Marshall, the Supreme Court established judicial review, safeguarding constitutional principles.
  • Struck down attempts to interfere with private contracts and limit interstate trade

Expansion of Political Rights
...

  • Over time, property requirements for voting were eliminated, marking progress in political rights.
  • Progressive Era reforms in the early 1900s introduced popular votes for Senators and other changes.

Advancements in Voting Rights
...

  • Women gained voting rights in the late 1800s and early 1900s, nationally recognized in 1920.
  • Slavery abolished in the 1860s; Jim Crow laws, disenfranchising blacks, struck down in the 1960s.

Temporary Restrictions During Crises
...

  • Wars and crises led to temporary restrictions on freedom for specific groups.
  • Post-1900, government regulation and social insurance created tensions with individual economic freedoms.

Leadership in Democracy and Economic Freedoms
...

  • US and Canada, particularly after 1870, led in democracy, economic freedom, property rights, and rule of law.
  • High levels of income and wealth correlated with economic freedoms, property rights, and unbiased rule of law.

Theoretical Models and Economic Development
...

  • Theoretical models and empirical analyses suggest economic freedoms, property rights, and rule of law contributed to advanced development.
  • Countries with these factors boast the highest per capita incomes globally.

Agricultural and Early Industrial Landscape (Early 1800s)
...

Agricultural Dominance (P 89)
...

  • Early 1800s: Over 80% of North American population engaged in agriculture.
  • Manufacturing focused on initial raw material transformations, with textiles, lumber, and footwear leading.

Textile Manufacturing and Innovation
...

  • Borrowed English textile methods; American textiles considered inferior until after 1865.
  • Jefferson's embargoes and high textile tariffs protected American manufacturers pre-1865.

Innovations and Adaptations (Pre-1865)
...

  • American innovators adapted foreign techniques; Eli Whitney pioneered manufacturing with replaceable parts.
  • Pennsylvania exploited coal and oil deposits in the late 1850s for fuel.

Resource Exploitation and Economic Expansion
...

  • Rich resource endowments, including wood, land, coal, and oil, fueled rapid economic expansion.
  • Map 3.2 displays coal and economic activities in mining and industrial production in the US c. 1890.

Territorial Expansion and Economic Shifts (1803-1860) (P 90)
...

Land Acquisitions
...

  • Louisiana Territory (1803), Florida (1819), Gadsden Purchase (1850s), and Alaska (1860s) expanded US territory.
  • Annexation of Texas and the Mexican–American War (1848) led to south-west and California acquisitions.

Westward Migration and Economic Transformation
...

  • Westward migration increased with ample available land, impacting the farm population share by 1860.
  • Farming declined to approximately 50%, while manufacturing, services, and transportation sectors expanded rapidly.

Cotton Production and Slave Economy (1808-1860)
...

  • Cotton production drove agricultural expansion in the southern US, constituting a significant export.
  • Despite the ban on importing slaves after 1808, the slave population in the south grew rapidly.

Economic Disparities and Per Capita Incomes
...

  • Southern economy, often perceived as backward due to reliance on slavery, had high per capita incomes.
  • Slave owners in the south ranked among the world's wealthiest despite economic perceptions.

Irony of Slavery in a Freedom-Leading Nation
...

  • Paradoxically, in a nation championing individual freedoms, the south heavily relied on slave labor.
  • Slaves' living conditions, though relatively better, were marked by a lack of freedom.

Post-Civil War Challenges and Inequality
...

  • Limited slave education, low post-war wealth, and unequal postwar economic settings contributed to low ex-slave incomes.
  • Post-Civil War conditions led to prolonged income disparities for ex-slaves and their descendants.

US Territorial Expansion (1803-1860)
...

Land Acquisitions
...

  • Louisiana Territory (1803), Florida (1819), Gadsden Purchase (1850s), and Alaska (1860s) expanded US territory (source: Unspecified).
  • Annexation of Texas and Mexican–American War (1848) secured southwest and California.

Westward Migration and Economic Shift
...

  • Westward migration along latitudes due to ample land availability.
  • Farm population share declined from over 80% to about 50% by 1860.

Cotton Production and Economic Impact
...

  • Cotton production fueled westward agriculture in the south, dominating American exports (source: North 1966).
  • Despite a ban on importing slaves after 1808, the slave population in the south grew rapidly.

Southern Economy and Per Capita Incomes
...

  • Despite perceptions of backwardness, southern per capita incomes were among the world's highest.
  • Slave owners in the south ranked among the wealthiest globally.

Irony of Slavery in a Freedom-Leading Nation
...

  • Paradoxically, in a freedom-leading nation, a large region relied on slave labor.
  • Slave conditions were relatively better than in Caribbean societies but lacked freedom (sources: Eltis 2000, Fogel 1994).

Post-Civil War Challenges and Inequality
...

  • Limited slave education and low post-war wealth contributed to ex-slave and offspring low incomes (sources: Higgs 1977, Margo 1990, Wright 2013).

Population growth 1800-1940: (P 91)
...

Economic Growth Factors
...

  • Population Expansion: Economic growth linked to population expansion, abundant land, and farming reliance.

Colonial Period to 1930s Population Growth
...

  • Declining Growth Rate: Progressively decreased from 3–3.5% (colonial era) to 2% (1900) (Atack and Passell 1994).
  • Birth Rate Decline: Crude birth rate dropped from 55 (c. 1800) to 19 (1940) (Atack and Passell 1994).
  • Farming Influence: Farm birth rates remained higher but declined due to technological changes (Haines 2000).

Long-Term Death Rate Trends (1800 - 1940)
...

  • Continuous Decline: Decreased from 25 (1800) to 11 (1940) with fluctuations.
  • 1918 Influenza Spike: Short-term spike from 14 to 18 per thousand during the 1918 influenza epidemic.
  • Infant Mortality Decline: Fell from over 200 (pre-1850) to 43 (1940).

Post-Civil War Improvements (After 1865)
...

  • Diet and Living Standards: Decline linked to improved diets and living standards after the Civil War.
  • Sanitary Facilities: Water treatment and sanitary facilities led to dramatic death rate drops after 1890.
  • Health Education: Basic public health education, handwashing, sterilization contributed to declines.
  • Medical Advancements: Significant medical improvements after 1920 with new vaccines and pasteurization (Atack and Passell 1994).

Post-Civil War Immigration and Economic Growth (1865 - 1910) (P 92)
...

  • High Earnings Attraction: Post-Civil War, high earnings potential attracted large immigrant numbers to America and Canada.
  • Real GDP Per Capita Disparity: Figure 3.3 shows higher real GDP per capita in Canada and the US than in Europe.
  • Earnings Disparity (1910): Annual earnings in US manufacturing significantly surpassed those in Europe (Rosenbloom 2002).
  • Dominant Immigrant Groups (Pre-1890): English, Irish, Welsh, Scottish, Germans, French, and Scandinavians were dominant immigrant groups.
  • Skill Transfer: Many immigrants brought mining and manufacturing skills, teaching them to American workers.
  • Entrepreneurial Impact: A large share of immigrants became entrepreneurs, contributing to industrial growth.
  • Shift from Farms (After 1880): Immigrants from southern and eastern Europe shifted from farms to North American industries.
  • Return and Entrepreneurship: Roughly one-third returned home to start farms or businesses with accumulated earnings.
  • Industrial Expansion Driver: Immigrants who stayed played a central role in the rapid expansion of the US industrial sectors.

Average Adult Height as a Measure of Living Standards (19th Century) (P 93)
...

  • Nutrition Indicator: Average adult height serves as an indicator of relative standards of living, reflecting childhood nutrition.
  • Canada and US Heights (Figure 3.4): In the 19th century, heights in Canada and the US were substantially higher than global averages.
  • Consistent with Earnings: Higher heights align with better living standards, consistent with earnings differences.
  • Decline in Heights (Late 19th Century): Heights in both countries declined in the latter half of the 19th century.
  • Contrast with Western Europe: While heights in Canada and the US decreased, western Europe experienced growth.
  • Less Dramatic Height Differences (1890, 1900): Height differences in 1890 and 1900 were not as dramatic as earnings and GDP per capita disparities.

Immigration Boom and Restrictions (Late 19th to 20th Century) (P 93)
...

  • Economic and Political Restrictions:
    • Chinese and Japanese Exclusion (1882, 1907): Federal laws restricted Chinese (1882) and Japanese (1907) immigration to the US (Cohn 2002).
    • First World War Impact: Immigration slowed significantly during the First World War.
    • Nativist Sentiment (Early 1920s): Nativist sentiment led to immigration restrictions, especially targeting southern and eastern Europe.
  • Recent Trends (Last 30 Years):
    • Sharp Rise in Immigration: Recent decades saw a sharp increase in the immigrant share of the population.
    • Flows from Latin America: Large legal and illegal flows from Latin America contributed to the rise (Cohn 2002).
    • Refugees and East Asia: Acceptance of refugees and expanded immigration from East Asia played a role.
  • Post-9/11 Restrictions:
    • Impact of Terrorism (2001): Post-9/11, new restrictions aimed to prevent terrorist attacks affected immigration flows.

Long Run Per Capital income Growth (p 94)
...

Economic Growth and Welfare Trends (1840-1860)
...

  • Conjectural estimates suggest income per capita growth rose from 0.5% to 1% annually (Baten, Crayen and Voth 2014).
  • Height decline (1820-1860) indicates potential health issues in urban and trading areas.

Long-Term Growth Resilience (1840 Onward)
...

  • Despite downturns, the US maintained a 1.6% annual growth, doubling income every 40 years.
  • US and Canada stand out with sustained living standards growth amid global challenges (North et al. 2009).

Agricultural Productivity Innovations (1800-1930)
...

  • National productivity slow but masks diverse innovations.
  • Seed experiments led to new crops; cotton hybridization increased harvest efficiency.
  • New wheat strains fueled a Canadian and Great Plains wheat boom.
  • Livestock cross-breeding expanded meat, leather, and wool yields.
  • Ongoing efforts to combat crop diseases required constant method and seed development.
  • Post-1840, steel ploughs, mechanical reapers, and new implements increased acreage and spurred industry (Olmstead and Rhode 2008).

Agricultural Contributions to Industrial Expansion (P 95)
...

  • Farmers' increased productivity and innovations fueled remarkable industrial growth.
  • Immigrant labor, technological advancements, and expanded food production played key roles.

Industrial Expansion and Coal-Powered Boom
...

  • Industrial growth, initiated pre-Civil War, accelerated with coal as a driving force.
  • Mining activity, linked to business cycles, remains influential in the modern era.

Transportation Revolution (1840-1910)
...

  • Railroad expansions (1840-1910) significantly reduced national transportation costs.
  • Canals and riverboats remained competitive, contributing to ongoing transportation innovations.

Financial Impact of Railroad Investments
...

  • Swings in railroad investments influenced financial markets, contributing to major crises.
  • Global implications arose as British and European investors heavily invested in American stocks and bonds.

US Steel Dominance and Technological Advancements
...

  • Rapid US steel capacity growth post-1860 led to world leadership by 1910.
  • Technological advancements (telephones, electric lights, automobiles, oil refining) improved living standards.

Industrial Consolidation and Anti-Trust Actions
...

  • Firms capitalized on economies of scale and better communication, growing larger.
  • Despite fears of monopoly, many firms lowered prices and increased output.
  • Trust-busting actions in early 1900s, including the break-up of Standard Oil and American Tobacco (1911).

Labor and Economic Success
...

  • Large firms offered high wages, making workers among the world's best-paid.
  • Limited union representation, but some gains through collective action.
  • US labor law changes in 1935 marked a shift in recognizing unions.

Interconnected Economies (1865-1914) (P 95)
...

  • Increased global integration between the US, Canada, and the world economy.
  • Flow of workers and financial capital fueled economic expansions.

Globalization Halt and Financial Turmoil (1914-1945)
...

  • First World War disrupted globalization, followed by a contraction during the Great Depression and Second World War.
  • Enormous debts, inflation, and reparations payments post-First World War contributed to international financial market problems.

Post-War Economic Challenges (1918-1929)
...

  • US returned to the gold standard post-World War I, leading to a patchwork version that unraveled in the 1930s.
  • Global issues, including debts and inflation, contributed to the Great Depression in the 1930s.

Economic Growth and Contractions (1920-1932)
...

  • Rapid domestic economic growth post-First World War until 1929.
  • Farming, coal mining, and shipbuilding declined after World War I expansions.
  • Housing boom (1920-1926) followed by declines in construction and housing values.
  • Recession in 1929 worsened by the stock market crash, Hawley-Smoot Tariff Act, and bank failures.

Federal Reserve Challenges and Economic Consequences (1930-1935)
...

  • Federal Reserve faced challenges with bank failures, the Hawley-Smoot Tariff Act, and gold standard concerns.
  • Delayed response to banking crises until spring 1932 led to a series of large-scale bond purchases.
  • Numerous bank failures, credit freeze, and deflation contributed to a severe economic downturn in 1933.
  • Real output dropped to 70% of the 1929 level, and unemployment rates exceeded 20% from 1932 through 1935.

Banking System Challenges (US vs. Canada) (P 97)
...

  • US banks faced size constraints due to state regulations on branch banking.
  • Canada's large-scale banks with a nationwide presence mitigated local economic shocks (Bordo et al. 1995).

Prolonged Depression and Policy Struggles (1930s)
...

  • Despite Hoover's and Roosevelt's efforts, the US remained in Depression throughout the 1930s.
  • Real output improved post-gold standard, but recovery was slow, with setbacks in 1937–38 (Fishback 2010).

Impact of World War II on Economic Recovery
...

  • World War II initially increased employment but did not improve consumer welfare.
  • Consumption sacrifices persisted; true economic recovery occurred post-war (Higgs 1992, 1999; Edelstein 2000).

The Destructiveness of wars
...

Economic Fallacy of Wars (p 97)
...

  • Misguided belief: Wars benefit the economy (limited experience with on-home soil wars).
  • Civil War (1861-1865) costs, equivalent to a year of GDP, had major repercussions.

Civil War Costs and Missed Opportunities
...

  • Civil War costs included significant expenditures and life losses.
  • The primary benefit was slave emancipation, but the economic cost was enormous.
  • An alternative peaceful settlement could have bought all slaves, provided land and capital, and paid back wages (Goldin and Lewis 1975).

North American Fortunes in World Wars (P 98)
...

  • North America fared better in both world wars, avoiding on-home soil conflicts.
  • Late entries and active roles contributed to turning the tide for allies.

Global Impact and North American Growth
...

  • 55-100 million lives lost outside North America; immense physical capital destruction.
  • Sharp spikes in real per capita GDP ratios show North American prosperity post-war.
  • Western Europe took years to recover post-World War II, contrasting rapid North American growth.

Sacrifices and Economic Shifts
...

  • US government shifted to war production before entering each conflict.
  • Nearly 40% of US GDP focused on war production during the Second World War peak.
  • Wartime decisions impacted large economic sectors; goods were rationed, prices controlled.
  • Consumer spending per capita remained stagnant or declined, especially considering rationing.
  • Workforce induction into the military, half wages, and wartime hazards characterized sacrifice.

Economic Winners and Common Sacrifice
...

  • Stock prices rose; some experienced wage and savings increases at home.
  • Opportunities expanded for minorities and women during wartime.
  • Post-war, rapid consumption and investment were driven by missed war opportunities (Higgs 1992, 1999; Edelstein 2000).

Post War Ear (P 98)
...

Post-World War II Defense Dynamics (P 98)
...

  • US spent significantly on non-Communist world defense post-World War II.
  • Share of US GDP for defense peaked in the 1950s, declining thereafter.

Cold War and Nuclear Arms Race (P 99)
...

  • Arms race unfolded between USSR and US/NATO during the Cold War.
  • Attempts to reduce missile numbers through treaty agreements occurred.

Military Engagements and Reagan Era
...

  • US combated Communism in Korea, Vietnam, Southeast Asia, and other global hotspots.
  • Ronald Reagan's presidency in the 1980s saw increased defense spending and the pursuit of a missile defense system.
  • Soviet Union's focus on military spending proved unsustainable; Cold War ended with Gorbachev's reforms.

Post-Cold War Policing and Challenges
...

  • US continued policing role post-Cold War; intervention in Iraq's invasion of Kuwait (1990).
  • After Gulf War, the US faced the challenge of allocating the 'peace dividend.'
  • 9/11 led to the declaration of a 'war on terror,' with military interventions in Afghanistan and Iraq.
  • Combination of tax cuts, increased government spending, and wars led to budget deficits (2001-2008).

Productivity advanceds after the wars (p 99)
...

Domestic Economic Transformations
...

  • Two rapid productivity growth eras: 1950s-1970s and late 1980s-mid-2000s.
  • Twentieth-century inventions and innovations transformed daily life, driven by mechanical and scientific advancements.

Technological Advancements (P 100)
...

  • Engineering breakthroughs led to innovations from radios to smartphones and internet connectivity.
  • Cross-disciplinary scientific advancements enabled medical breakthroughs like transplants, surgeries, and cancer treatments.

Human Capital Development
...

  • Productivity gains tied to the development of human capital.
  • Both US and Canada prioritized education, leading in numeracy measures and expanding access to high school and university education.
  • Widespread opportunities for higher education with increasing college graduate rates.

Global Impact of Educational Excellence (p 101)
...

  • US and Canada developed high-quality universities, attracting global talent.
  • Foreign students contribute significantly, becoming leading entrepreneurs in North American companies.
  • While North America led in education for much of the 20th century, global competition in university education is rising (Goldin and Katz 2008, Field 2011).

The Business cycle (P101)
...

Historical Patterns of the US Economy
...

  • US economy characterized by long-term per capita income growth with occasional downturns.
  • The Great Depression of the 1930s marked a severe drop in percentage output.

Fluctuations and Financial Crises
...

  • Numerous fluctuations and financial crises occurred, with major downturns in various periods.
  • Downturns in the 1830s, 1870s, 1890s, 1907–08, late 1970s, early 1980s, and 2007–09.

Financial Sector Challenges
...

  • Financial sector problems associated with downturns; causation sometimes real to financial, sometimes vice versa.

Strong Banking Systems in North America
...

  • US and Canada maintained robust banking and financial systems.
  • Bank runs were often narrowly targeted and did not lead to broad-based runs.
  • Clearinghouses played a role in stability during crises, ensuring stronger banks provided liquidity.
  • Bank failures often were suspensions of redemptions or withdrawals, not complete losses (Wicker 2000).

Post-WWII Economic Policies (P 102)
...

  • Reemployment Act of 1946 aimed for economic growth, low unemployment, and low inflation.
  • Successful performance from 1947 to 1969: 2.6% annual GDP per capita growth, low inflation, 4.7% average unemployment.

Economic Confidence in the 1960s
...

  • Confidence in smoothing business cycles with Keynesian fiscal policy grew.
  • Kennedy tax cut in the early 1960s stimulated the economy, with a smaller deficit during the cut.

Economic Challenges in the 1970s
...

  • In the 1970s, stagflation (high unemployment and inflation) contradicted the Phillips curve.
  • Nixon administration's wage and price controls had limited impact on inflation.
  • OPEC doubled oil prices, and attempts to reduce unemployment led to further inflation.

Volcker's Monetary Policy in the 1980s
...

  • Paul Volcker's tightened monetary policy in the early 1980s reduced inflationary expectations.
  • Reagan administration aimed to stimulate aggregate supply by cutting tax rates and pushing deregulation.
  • Budget deficits in the Reagan and first Bush administration ranged between 3 and 6 percent of GDP.

Post-2008 Economic Landscape (P 103)
...

  • Long Expansions and Setbacks (1982-2007):

    • Three of the five longest expansions in U.S. history.
    • Real per capita GDP grew at 2.1% annually.
    • Stock market boomed, internet and 'dot com' companies reshaped information and marketing.

  • Post-Dot Com Bust and Housing Shift:

    • After the dot com bust, low-interest rates led to increased investment in housing.
    • Policies encouraged riskier mortgage loans to boost home ownership.
    • Complex financial instruments (MBSs, CDOs, CDSs) were developed to diversify and limit risk.

  • Housing Market Collapse (2006):

    • Housing prices started tumbling in 2006, exposing vulnerabilities in the financial system.
    • Recession began in December 2007, leading to a sharp decline in the stock market.
    • Government interventions and bailouts were employed to stabilize the financial sector.

  • Recession Fallout and Recovery (2008-2014):

    • Official exit from recession in early 2009, but high unemployment persisted.
    • Keynesian stimulus package doubled the deficit but did not fully resolve unemployment.
    • Federal Reserve interventions, low-interest rates, and low inflation characterized the post-recession period.
    • S&P 500 reached record highs by 2014, but housing issues and tight credit persisted.
    • Slow recovery influenced by global factors, including sovereign debt problems in the Eurozone.

Post-2008 Economic Challenges (p 103-p104)
...

  • Persistent Unemployment:

    • Unemployment remained above 8% until 2014, hitting a peak of 9% in 2011.
    • The employment rate stayed below pre-recession levels throughout 2014.

  • Keynesian Stimulus Impact:

    • Despite a substantial Keynesian stimulus, unemployment problems persisted.
    • Federal Reserve liquidity injections, record-low interest rates, and low inflation marked the economic landscape.

  • Global Factors and Slow Recovery:

    • Global issues, such as sovereign debt problems in the Eurozone, contributed to a slow U.S. recovery.
    • Housing problems and lingering impacts on banks continued to affect credit availability.

Growth of government (p 104-105)

Evolution of Government Role in 20th Century (P 104)
...

  • Circa 1900:

    • Limited government influence on economic decisions.
    • Local and state regulations were primary factors.
    • Opinion leaders favored minimal reliance on government support.

  • Post-World Wars and Great Depression:

    • Federal government expanded significantly in response to crises.
    • Expanded roles in regulation and economic activities.
    • Retraction post-crises, but at higher levels than before.

  • Post-Expansion Trends:

    • Changes driven by regulations and transfer payments rather than direct consumption.
    • Despite sporadic deregulation in the late 20th century, federal regulations continued to expand.
    • Welfare state growth, especially in social insurance, became a dominant trend.

  • Social Welfare and Healthcare:

    • Pure transfers to the poor increased from 1% to 3% of GDP.
    • Growth in health care expenditures, notably Medicaid, contributed to the welfare state.
    • Private spending on health care, often through employers, distinguishes the U.S. from other countries.
    • Total social welfare spending comparable to or exceeding other nations when private healthcare spending is considered.

Challenges in Public Social Insurance (P 105)
...

  • Key Expansions:
    • Social Security (1935):
      • Pay-as-you-go program.
      • Taxes fund pensions, invested in government bonds.
    • Medicare (1964):
      • Similar structure for elderly health care.
  • Demographic Challenges:
    • Baby boom (1957) and increased life expectancy.
    • Rising costs due to demographic shifts.
    • Challenges in maintaining worker-to-retiree ratios.
  • Financial Dynamics:
    • Taxes collected exceeded benefits due to rate increases.
    • Social Security tax rates rose from 1% (1940) to over 6% (1988).
    • Future challenges with more baby boomers reaching retirement.
    • Long-run budget issues may lead to tax hikes or benefit reductions.
  • Comparison with Other Regions:
    • US and Canada in a comparatively better position.
    • Faster-growing working-age population.
    • Western European nations face larger challenges with lower worker ratios.
    • Greece's sovereign debt issues in 2011 highlight problems with social insurance in Europe.
  • Future Scenarios:
    • Continued expansion of taxes and transfers or retractions in benefits likely.
    • Potential challenges similar to those experienced by Greece may emerge in other regions.
