9 Southeast Asia and Australia, New Zealand (Martin Shanahan) P 282

Geography and latitude
...

Economic and Social Diversity in Southeast Asia
...

Country Spectrum
...

  • Highest Living Standards: Australia, New Zealand, Singapore.
  • Poorest Nations: Papua New Guinea, Timor-Leste.
  • Middle-Ranked Countries: Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia, Philippines, Vietnam, Laos, Cambodia.

Historical Transformations
...

  • Diverse Societal Changes: Overthrowing colonialism, wars, economic shifts.
  • Cultural Origins: Rooted in ancient civilizations, shaping modern identities.

Rapid Economic Transformation
...

  • Asian Tigers: Notable economic progress in the second half of the 20th century.
  • Deep Cultural Roots: Despite modernization, rooted in ancient civilizations.

Influence of Colonial Rule
...

  • European Domination: Shaped economic attitudes and policies.
  • Legacy of Wars and Independence: Continued impact on socio-economic conditions.

Role of Trade
...

  • Tradition of Trading: Long history of trade shaping economic well-being.
  • Post-War Progress: Rise in GDP per capita, life expectancy, and heights linked to trade.

Importance of Stability
...

  • Institutional and Political Stability: Crucial for economic advancement.
  • Violence and Development: Lower violence correlates with higher material outcomes.

Self-Reinforcing Improvement
...

  • Education and Life Expectancy: Economic growth fuels investment in education and health.
  • Evidence from Southeast Asia: Demonstrates the self-reinforcing cycle of economic development.

Geographic and Cultural Landscape of Southeast Asia
...

Regional Clusters
...

  • Mainland Southeast Asia: Includes Cambodia, Laos, Myanmar, Malaysia, and Singapore.
  • Australasia: Encompasses Australia and New Zealand.
  • Island Nations: Timor-Leste, Indonesia, Papua New Guinea, and the Philippines.

Diversity in Size and Population
...

  • Vast Differences: Singapore's 700 square kilometers vs. Australia's 7.7 million square kilometers.
  • Population Contrast: Timor-Leste (fewer than 1.2 million) vs. Indonesia (over 250 million).

Geographic Extent
...

  • Latitude and Longitude: Spanning 75 degrees of latitude and 86 degrees of longitude.
  • Diverse Landscapes: Deserts, tropical forests, marshlands, plains, and mountains.

Terrain and Water Resources
...

  • Basins and River Systems: Influential rivers like the Irrawaddy, Mekong, and Red.
  • Contrast in Stability: Stable Thai-Malay peninsula vs. volcanic areas like Indonesia.

Water Resources and Population
...

  • Abundance of Water: 15% of the world's renewable water resources.
  • Population Distribution: Unevenly spread across various landscapes and climates.

Agricultural Diversity
...

  • Variety of Farming: From dairy farming in New Zealand to rice fields in Vietnam.
  • Influencing Factors: Varying soil fertility, water availability, and climate.

Cultural Influences
...

  • Religious and Governance Mix: Hindu-Buddhist traditions in Burma, Thailand, Cambodia, and Laos.
  • Islamic Influence: Dominant in Indonesia, parts of Malaysia, and coastal areas.
  • Christianity: Dominant in the Philippines after Spanish colonization in the 16th century.

Pre-Colonial Southeast Asia (1500–1800) (P 284)
...

Relative Stability
...

  • Comparative Calm: Contrasted with the turbulent colonial period that followed.
  • Complex Society: Mix of simple and sophisticated societies, diverse languages, and religions.

Social Structure
...

  • Economic Disparity: Wide gap between subsistence farmers and ruling elite.
  • Absence of Middle Class: Predominance of peasant farming, with no middle-class existence.
  • Comparison to Europe: Similarities in societal structure, except in Australasia.

European Trade
...

  • Trading Ports: Europeans familiar with trading ports like Cambay, Melaka, and Macao.
  • Gradual Contact: Initial contact with limited impact beyond port regions and hinterlands.
  • Mercantilism: European states vied for control, leading to increased trade interests.
  • Colonial Expansion: Spaniards in the Philippines, Dutch in Indonesia, and British in India.

Traditional Life
...

  • Limited Interaction: Majority lived away from ports, little European interest in local affairs.
  • Continuance of Social Routines: Traditional life continued for most despite European presence.
  • Hardship: Subsistence agriculture, fishing, and hunting prevailed in less developed civilizations.
  • Regional Differences: Lowlands more connected socially and economically; uplands less inhabited.
  • Ethnic Diversity: Dominant ethnic groups in some regions, while others had diverse populations.
  • Tribal Communities: Tribes inhabited further-flung regions like parts of the Philippines and Australia.

New Imperialism and the Colonial Period (P 287)
...

Shift to Empire Building
...

  • Transformation of Ideas: European shift from mercantilism to empire building in 18th-19th centuries.
  • War and Industrial Transformation: Reshaped European attitudes and capabilities.

European Expansion in Southeast Asia
...

  • British Expansion in Burma: Control proclaimed after three successful wars.
  • French Expansion: Invasion of Vietnam and possession of Cambodia and Laos.
  • Dutch Influence in Indonesia: Dutch East India Company's influence spread across the archipelago.
  • Spanish Colonization: Possession of the Philippines, challenged frequently, lost to the US in 1898.
  • British Dominion: Control over the Malay Peninsula, Australia, and New Zealand.

Economic Transformation
...

  • Commodity Production: Dairy farms in New Zealand, rubber plantations in Malaysia, tin mining in Malaya.
  • Labor and Capital: Imported labor and capital transformed production techniques.
  • Transportation Revolution: From sail to steam, animal-drawn carts to railways, enabling global trade expansion.
  • Communication Advances: Improved communication facilitated trade growth.
  • Role of Suez Canal: Lowered costs between Europe and Southeast Asia, strengthening trading ports.

Impact on Southeast Asian Economies
...

  • Integration into Global Markets: Supplying commodities to industrialized countries.
  • Technological Advancement: Adoption of modern production methods.
  • Labor and Capital Influx: Foreign investment and imported labor contributed to economic growth.

Migration and Colonial Legacies
...

Migration in the Nineteenth Century
...

  • Movement to New World: Significant migration to Australia and New Zealand, especially after gold discovery.
  • Chinese Migration: Rapid influx into Singapore and supply of labor for tin mines in Malaya.
  • Indian Migration: Migration into Burma and Malaysia-Singapore.

Colonial Legacy
...

  • National Boundaries: Colonialism shaped today's national boundaries.
  • Standardization of Record Keeping: Introduction of internationally recognized laws and administrative processes.
  • Awareness of Outside World: Increased awareness among local populations due to reduced control over affairs.
  • Aspirations of Elites: Growing awareness of Western education's importance for accessing trade and political positions.

Economic and Political Consequences
...

  • Unequal Distribution: Economic growth didn't ensure equitable resource distribution.
  • Political Transformation: Transition to independence varied across regions.

Contrasting Paths to Independence
...

  • Australia and New Zealand: Peaceful political transformation led to independence from Britain.
  • Southeast Asia: More difficult path due to local populations' disenfranchisement and colonial power control.
  • Impact of External Factors: Economic pressure and events like the depression and World Wars influenced political change.
  • Path to Independence: Varied negotiations and conflicts led to independence for each country.

Post-Independence Governance
...

  • Stability and Democracy: Australia and New Zealand had stable democratic institutions.
  • Improvements Over Time: Institutions improved, albeit slowly, in other Southeast Asian nations in the second half of the twentieth century.

Variations in Living Standards
...

  • Diverse Paths to Independence: Different paths and responses to challenges explain variations in living standards post-World War II.

Post Second World War (P 292)
...

Return to Open Trade
...

  • Global Trends: Return to open trade and international cooperation after World War II.
  • Southeast Asia's Legacy: Long legacy of international trade facilitated post-war improvements.

Emergence of Asian Tigers
...

  • Singapore's Transformation: Emergence as one of the Asian Tigers, along with Hong Kong, South Korea, and Taiwan.
  • High Growth Rates: Rapid growth rates between 1965 and 1995 transformed living standards in these economies.

High Performing Asian Economies (HPAE)
...

  • Factors for Rapid Growth: Trade openness, financial stability, technology, human capital, and interventionist policies.
  • Diverse Growth Trajectories: Indonesia, Malaysia, and Thailand achieved significant growth with equitable outcomes.

Formation of ASEAN
...

  • Regional Cooperation: ASEAN formed in 1967, gradually expanded to include more countries, promoting regional stability and economic growth.

Urbanization and Industrialization
...

  • Shift to Cities: Massive rural-to-urban migration, especially in Indonesia, the Philippines, Thailand, Vietnam, and Malaysia.
  • Urbanization Challenges: Concentration of unskilled labor brings challenges of poverty, crime, and inadequate infrastructure.

Economic Development Trends
...

  • GDP per Capita: Australia, New Zealand, and Singapore saw rapid improvements, while other countries like Malaysia and Thailand showed significant growth.
  • Transformation in Living Standards: Collective improvements have transformed the lives and opportunities of millions.

Improvements in Life Expectancy: A Measure of Living Standards
...

Historical Context
...

  • Before 1850s, European life expectancy: 30-35 years; Philippines lagged behind in 20th century.
    • Indonesia's average below 30 in 1940s; Timor-Leste in 1950s.
  • Rapid 20th-century improvements shown in Figure 9.4.
    • Notable dimension of Southeast Asian living standards enhancement.

Regional Comparisons
...

  • Mid-20th century:
    • Myanmar, Laos, Cambodia, Vietnam: life expectancies akin to 19th-century developed nations.
  • 1960s:
    • Papua New Guinea, Timor-Leste: under 40 years; recent significant improvements.
  • Disparities persist:
    • Infants in wealthier countries have over a decade's advantage.
    • Cambodia, Timor-Leste: face lifetimes two decades shorter than wealthiest nations' contemporaries.

Education and Economic Development
...

Importance of Education
...

  • Education vital for future living standards and average life expectancy.
  • Sharp increase in average years of education post-1950s evident in most Southeast Asian nations.
  • Slow improvement in Cambodia's education suggests challenges for future.

Government Policy and Investment
...

  • Education investment crucial for economic benefits, though long-term.
  • Priority on basic primary education, but potential for highly educated workforce.
  • Government commitment and investment can yield significant educational improvements.

Economic Transformation and Trade
...

Evolution of Economies
...

  • Shift from labor-intensive manufacturing to regionally integrated supply chains.
  • Many economies transitioned from importing capital to exporting capital.
  • Timor-Leste and Papua New Guinea still reliant on external aid for infrastructure.

Economic Adjustments in Developed Nations
...

  • Australia, New Zealand, and Singapore faced economic adjustments despite high living standards.
  • Lowering tariff barriers, currency adjustments facilitated economic growth.

Role of Trade in Advancing Living Standards
...

  • Trade openness critical for economic growth and living standards.
  • Nations with high export levels per capita prospered.
  • Rapid improvement in export performance seen in Thailand, Vietnam.
  • Timor-Leste's low export value suggests ongoing challenges but hope for improvement.

Concluding Remarks
...

Generational Transformation
...

  • Post-1960s world vastly different from that of previous generations.
  • Wide disparities exist in living standards across Southeast Asia and Oceania.

Achievements and Transformations
...

  • Australia, New Zealand: Maintained highest living standards for over a century.
  • Singapore, Malaysia, Philippines, Indonesia, Thailand: Dramatic improvements from below global average to above.
  • Progress in Thailand, Vietnam, Cambodia, Laos, especially recent years, though challenges persist.
  • Papua New Guinea, Myanmar, Timor-Leste: Lagging behind, reminiscent of earlier times.

Impact and Potential
...

  • These countries represent 10% of the world's population.
  • Government policy changes can significantly impact millions of lives.
  • Various influences shape growth paths: trade, war, governance, education, etc.

Common Building Blocks for Improvement
...

  • Peace, trade opportunities, good governance, property rights, education, health crucial.
  • Understanding history and lessons crucial for addressing present and future challenges.
