Japan's Demographic Development (p 168)
...

  • Japan's insular geography insulated it from disease contacts (McNeill 1979: 133).
  • Dense populations developed, vulnerable to severe epidemics (McNeill 1979: 133-34).
  • McNeillian trap affected Japan and other peripheral areas (McNeill 1979: 135-36).
  • Economic progress followed crossing demographic thresholds (McNeill 1979: 135).

Transition to Early Modernity (p 169)
...

  • Transition began in mid-sixteenth century, not early seventeenth (Hayami).
  • Famine frequency decline correlated with political consolidation (Saito 2015a).
  • Samurai power replaced central administration in medieval Japan.
  • Warlords' rule reduced excess mortality, aiding population growth.
  • Territorial consolidation facilitated agricultural progress (Saito 2015a).
  • Sixteenth-century state formation preceded Tokugawa regime.
  • Investments in land development boosted agricultural productivity (Saito 2015a).

Maritime Trade and Economic Growth (p 169-170)
...

  • Sixteenth-century population growth coincided with maritime trade boom.
  • Introduction of cupellation technology boosted silver exports (Hayami).
  • Wakō's seafaring trade expanded, involving various traders (Lucassen et al. 2014).
  • Population growth, state formation, and economic development intertwined.
  • Japan escaped the McNeillian trap by 1600, entering early modern growth.

Population Growth and Urbanization ( P 171-172)
...

  • Seventeenth-century population growth slower than previously estimated (Miyamoto 2004).
  • Arable land and farm output increased significantly (Miyamoto 2004).
  • Urbanization rose, with urban population tripling by 1720 (Lucassen et al. 2014).

Per Capita GDP Estimates (P 172)
...

  • Two estimates of pre-Meiji Restoration per capita GDP exist (Maddison 2001; Bassino et al. 2011).
  • Discrepancies exist, particularly regarding urbanization rates and trade policies (Totman 1993).
  • New method considers both urbanization and population density for better estimates (Takashima).
  • Proto-industrialization impacted secondary and tertiary sector output (Takashima).

Comparative Analysis (P 172)
...

  • Per capita GDP estimates align closely around 1700-1721 but diverge afterward (Takashima).
  • Our estimates show sustained growth, contrasting with Maddison's steady path (Takashima).

Tokugawa Society and Economic Growth (P 174)
...

  • Tokugawa society experienced close to zero GDP per capita growth (Smith 1959).
  • Mining and export boom pre-Tokugawa not fully reflected in output estimates (Smith 1959).
  • Revised GDP per capita change estimated at -0.01% annually (Smith 1959).
  • Negative growth partly due to moderate 0.5% population increase (Smith 1959).
  • Decline accelerated by silver export depletion and end of construction boom (Smith 1959).
  • Market economy stabilized post-1700 despite challenges (Smith 1959).
  • Living standards for peasants might not have necessarily worsened (Smith 1959).
  • Agrarian organization shifted to individual farm families' control (Smith 1958).
  • Agricultural intensification under Tokugawa taxation system benefitted peasants (Smith 1958).
  • Non-farm employment increased cash incomes for households (Smith 1958; Smith 1969; Nishikawa 1987).
  • Tokugawa's agrarian economy advance didn't equate to Western catch-up (Nishikawa 1987).
  • Per capita GDP in Japan lagged significantly behind the West by 1874 (Nishikawa 1987).

Tokugawa Society and Economic Growth (p 174)
...

  • Early Tokugawa society experienced near-zero GDP per capita growth (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Mining and export boom not fully reflected in GDP estimate for 1600 (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Revised estimate shows negative growth due to various factors (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Population growth contributed to negative growth (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Decline exacerbated by factors like silver depletion and end of construction boom (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Market economy stabilized after 1700 despite challenges (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Peasantry likely did not experience worsened living standards (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Farm families became central to production organization (Smith, 1959).
  • Intensification of agriculture and non-farm employment boosted incomes (Smith, 1958; Smith, 1969; Nishikawa, 1987).

Tokugawa's Economic Positioning (p 175)
...

  • Economic advancements didn't equate to catching up with the West (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Per capita GDP by 1874 was lower compared to the West(2,064 dollars) (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).
  • Japan's GDP surpassed China and India during the late 18th and early 19th centuries (Nakamura & Ōkazaki, 2001).

Economic Growth in Tokugawa Japan (p 176)
...

  • Economic growth in Tokugawa Japan occurred over a 150-year period, characterized by limited overseas trade and pre-modern methods of production, without machinery or factories (Smith, 1973).

Sectoral Composition
...

  • The tertiary sector, involving trade, finance, and commerce, played a significant role throughout the Tokugawa period, with its share larger than that of the secondary sector (Smith, 1973).

Structural Stability
...

  • Despite steady growth after 1720, there was little structural change in sectoral shares until the final sub-period (1846–74), indicating balanced output growth across rural industry, agriculture, commerce, and services (Smith, 1973).

Regional Specialization and Division of Labor (p 177)
...

  • Economic development led to regional specialization, exemplified by the separation of industrial crop cultivation from food crop cultivation and the rise of silk weaving, which increased demand for raw silk (Smith, 1973).
  • This specialization created more part-time or by-employment opportunities in transport and distribution, fostering Adam Smith's division of labor (Smith, 1973).

Rural-Centered Growth
...

  • Tokugawa Japan experienced "rural-centered" Smithian growth, characterized by deurbanization and regional specialization, unlike the "urban-centered" growth seen in other contexts (Smith, 1973).

Income Distribution and Social Equality (P 177)
...

  • Rural-centered development in Tokugawa Japan did not benefit samurai and urban wealthy, suggesting a leveling effect on income distribution (Smith, 1973).
  • Wage trends in Tokugawa Japan did not diverge from per capita GDP growth, unlike in north-western Europe where real wages tended to decline despite output growth (Saito, 2005a).
  • Interclass inequality was surprisingly low, as evidenced by a reconstruction of a social table for the Chōshū region in the 1840s (Saito, 2015b).

Literacy and Numeracy
...

  • Tokugawa Japan exhibited high levels of literacy and numeracy compared to other Eurasian countries (Ma, 2015; Dore, year).
  • The basic numeracy index in East Asia, including Japan, was comparable to that of north-west European countries, with Japan's index nearing 100% throughout the nineteenth century (Ma, 2015).
  • By the end of the Tokugawa period, approximately 43% of boys had received some form of schooling, contributing to widespread literacy (Dore, year).

Human Capital Investment
...

  • The significant advance in human capital investment during the Tokugawa period served as an important legacy for subsequent industrialization eras

Economic Growth in Meiji Japan
...

  • Japan's industrialization in the late 19th century aimed at achieving economic growth, leading to its success story in the 20th century (Allen 2011).
  • Per capita GDP growth was steady until 1935, with slight deviations during the World Wars and military buildup in the 1930s (Allen 2011).
  • Japan's growth performance before 1950 was average globally and not exceptionally superior in interwar East Asia (Ma et al.).
  • Catch-up with the West occurred between 1950 and 1973, with an average growth rate of 8.1% (Allen 2011).

Role of the Developmental State (p 179)
...

  • The term "developmental state" characterizes Japan's interventionist state focused on economic development (Johnson 1982).
  • Early Meiji policies and MITI's industrial policy post-World War II were considered effective, though some argue for minimal government guidance in the success of emerging industries (Saito 2014; Komiya et al. 1988).
  • The transition to consumer durables industries post-1950s marked Japan's industrial success (Fukao and Saito 2013).

Deceleration and Transition to a Service Economy
...

  • Economic growth slowed post-1973, with manufacturing sector share declining and a transition to a service economy (Fukao and Saito 2013).
  • Despite this transition, productivity in the tertiary sector remained low, posing challenges for sustained economic growth (Fukao and Saito 2013).
