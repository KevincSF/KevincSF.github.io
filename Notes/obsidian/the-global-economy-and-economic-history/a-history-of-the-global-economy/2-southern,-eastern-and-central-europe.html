  • Lack of coverage in economic history books despite exciting and dynamic histories.

  • Countries between Portugal and Russia receive less coverage despite significant development.

Racing Towards Progress (Progressive Regions)

  • Periods of development acceleration followed by regressions require exploration.

...

  • Spain and Italy had higher national incomes and education levels than the UK in 1500.

Skilled Worker Migration (Worker Migration)

  • Skilled workers migrated from north to south during the late medieval and early modern periods.
  • Italy, in particular, attracted skilled workers, challenging modern migration patterns.

  • Europe experienced a human capital revolution from 1450 to 1800.
  • Numeracy increased from 50% to nearly 100%, comparable to early 20th-century global differences.

Factors Contributing to Slower Italian Development
...

Military Conflicts and Political Fractionalization (Conflict and Fractionalization)
...

  • Foreign-initiated conflicts, political fractionalization, and limited fiscal capacity contributed to decline.
  • Hindered defense, leading to difficulties in maintaining city-states and principalities.

Shift of World Trade (Trade Shift)
...

  • Shift of world trade to north-western Europe disconnected Italians from new international routes.
  • Dutch and English dominance in merchant shipping marginalized Italian involvement.

Seventeenth Century Plague (Plague Impact)
...

  • Alfani (2013) argued the seventeenth-century plague affected Italy significantly.
  • This exogenous event might have contributed to Italy's relative decline.

Institutional Developments (Institutional Differences)
...

  • Institutional developments in north-western Europe were more beneficial than in the south.
  • Venice's aristocratic oligarchy hindered new entrepreneurs, limiting socio-economic development.

Standards of Living in Early Modern East-Central Europe
...

Surprising Standard of Living (High Living Standards)
...

  • East-central Europe experienced a high standard of living in the late 16th and early 17th centuries.
  • Notably, unskilled urban workers and some farmer groups enjoyed relatively healthy lives.

Human Stature and Real Wages (Indicators of Welfare)
...

  • Human stature and real wages are indicators of human welfare.
  • Eastern European males were taller on average than southern and northern European counterparts.

East-Central European Golden Age (Golden Age)
...

  • Eastern Europeans were an average of 171.4 cm tall, supporting the idea of a golden age.
  • Recent research by Malinowski (2013) notes comparable real wages to England c. 1600.

Factors Sustaining High Standards (Sustaining High Standards)
...

  • Low population density in late medieval period attracted settlers under favorable conditions.
  • Sixteenth-century constraints on Polish kings by parliament had positive effects on general welfare.

Shift in Governance and Economic Policies (Governance Shift) (P 47)
...

  • Seventeenth and eighteenth centuries saw a shift to extractive governance and rent-seeking by nobility.
  • Civil wars, epidemic diseases, and increased exploitation of peasants characterized this period.

Second Serfdom (Serfdom Impact)
...

  • Second serfdom during the sixteenth century led to increasing labor demands and peasant exploitation.
  • Detrimental effects became more visible in the seventeenth century due to wars and demands.

Export of Nutrition and Health (Export Dynamics)
...

  • Seventeenth-century policies led to the export of Polish grain to western Europe.
  • Nutrition and health worsened as grain was consumed outside the country, impacting the local population.

Russia's Early-Modern Challenges and Economic Structure (P 48)
...

  • Territorial Expansion: Principality of Moscow grew rapidly, covering European Russia (Bibikov 2013).
  • Military Threats: Faced constant challenges from the west, south, and east (Polish, Swedish, Crimean Tatars, Ottomans, pastoral tribes) (Bibikov 2013).
  • Economic Structure: Moscow aligned its economy with military needs, utilizing government-owned and private lands (pomest’e, votchina) to support the army (Bibikov 2013).

Demographics and Social Composition
...

  • Agricultural Focus: Russia was predominantly agricultural with minimal urban, clergy, and military/administrative representation (Bibikov 2013).
  • Slave Population: 5-10% were slaves, primarily in households, contributing to the agrarian labor force (Bibikov 2013).
  • Serfdom Development: Second serfdom gained momentum after the government restricted peasant mobility in 1592 (Bibikov 2013).

Implications for Russia's Development
...

  • Economic Impact: Moscow's economic structure influenced Russia's development and military capabilities (Bibikov 2013).
  • Nutritional Status: Despite favorable land-labor ratios, Russians had low nutritional statuses in the eighteenth century (Mironov 2012).
  • Human Capital and Modernization: Russia experienced a human capital revolution and economic modernization in the mid-nineteenth century (Mironov 2012).

Tectonic Shifts in Regional Development (16th-18th centuries)
...

  • Focus Regions: Italy, Poland, and Russia experienced significant development shifts during this period.

Early 19th Century Regional Disparities
...

  • Variety in Development: Substantial economic development variations existed among European regions around 1800.

Challenges in Assessing Early 19th Century Development
...

  • Unavailable Indicators: Key indicators like regional GDP, real wages, and life expectancy are unavailable for this period.
  • Numeracy as Indicator: Numeracy, a measure of education, is a crucial available indicator for European regions.

Numeracy as a Development Indicator (P 50)
...

  • Importance of Numeracy: Numeracy, a component of the Human Development Index (HDI), serves as a key development indicator.
  • Predictive Value: Education, especially math skills, predicts income, influencing the HDI's income component.

Regional Numeracy Disparities in Early 19th Century Italy
...

  • Divided Italy: Early 19th-century Italy displayed notable regional disparities in numeracy.
  • Southern Italy: Numeracy substantially lower, foreshadowing later arguments about increased differences post-Italian unification (Vecchi 2011).

Factors Influencing Numeracy Disparities
...

  • Land Inequality's Role:
    • Powerful elites hindered public education in regions with high land inequality.
    • Elite reluctance to finance education for manual laborers due to potential threats to their status.
  • Educational Policy Impact:
    • Government investment in education crucial; Ottoman Empire and Russian Empire lagged.
    • Limited public funds allocated to education in the Ottoman Empire (0.2% of total budget in 1860–61).
  • Other Determinants:
    • Proximity to trading ports, personal status (e.g., serfdom), and religion also influence numeracy.

Russian Empire's Regional Numeracy Differences
...

  • Baltic Region Influence:

    • Estonian and Latvian parts had higher numeracy, possibly due to Protestant religion (Becker and Woessmann 2009).
    • Hanseatic trade heritage in the Baltic region contributed to urban centers' development.

  • Regional Disparities:

    • Numeracy higher in north and northeast Russia than in central Russia and Belarus (Hippe and Baten 2014).
    • Improved legal position, less oppressive serfdom, and better economic situations in frontier settlements contributed to higher numeracy.

  • Land Inequality Impact:

    • Central Russia and Belarus had high land inequality, correlating with lower numeracy.

Habsburg Empire's Numeracy Variation
...

  • Empire Composition:

    • Habsburg Empire included modern Hungary, Austria, Czech Republic, Slovakia, parts of Poland, Italy, Romania, and former Yugoslavia.

  • Numeracy Levels:

    • Western Habsburg Empire had the highest numeracy; substantial variation existed within the empire.
    • Large estates predominance in some regions explained differences (Hippe and Baten 2014).

Ottoman Empire's Impact on Numeracy in Southern Balkans (P 50)
...

  • Low Numeracy Levels:

    • Southern Balkan countries, long under Ottoman rule, exhibit some of the lowest numeracy levels.
    • Ottoman Empire's small education budget; education seen as the responsibility of Islamic schools or local initiatives (Karaman and Pamuk 2010).

  • Budget Allocation Challenges:

    • Ottoman Empire had a low capacity to raise taxes; central budget prioritized military and bureaucracy.
    • Numeracy, school enrolment, and literacy rates remained low in Ottoman-controlled countries.

  • Post-Ottoman Persistence:

    • Some countries continued to experience low human capital levels even after Ottoman rule ended.
    • Greece and Cyprus faced challenges due to the paths taken under Ottoman educational policies (Figure 2.3).

  • Twentieth Century Improvement:

    • Greece and Cyprus saw rapid increases in numeracy during the twentieth century.

Caucasus: Armenia and Georgia
...

  • Imperial Influences:

    • Armenia and Georgia interacted with both Ottoman and Persian Empires during the early modern period.
    • Governed by Persian or Ottoman forces, sometimes with semi-autonomous status, before Russian territorial expansion.

  • Russian Conquest and Numeracy:

    • Russian Empire gradually conquered Armenia and Georgia in the late 18th and 19th centuries.
    • Low numeracy levels persisted, influenced by Ottoman and Persian educational policies that gradually improved during the Russian period.

Italy and Spain: North-South Disparities (P 51)
...

  • Human Capital Differences:
    • Strong north-south disparities in human capital observed in Italy and Spain.
    • Southern regions, specializing in grain, had low numeracy and high land inequality.
  • Industrial Growth Centers:
    • Northwestern Italy experienced industrial growth, particularly in Genoa, Milan, and Turin.
    • Traditional wool specialization in central Meseta region of Spain declined; Catalonia developed a cotton textile industry.

Central Europe: Industrialization and Numeracy (P 52)
...

  • Industrialization Centers:

    • Central Europe, including Rhineland, Ruhr area, and Silesia, had higher industrialization.
    • Focus on iron, mining, machinery, tools, and textiles during the early 19th century.

  • High Numeracy in Central Europe:

    • Switzerland, with a clock-making tradition, and other central European regions had high numeracy in the early 19th century.

European Region: Human Capital Formation
...

  • Land Inequality Impact:
    • Large estates correlated with less human capital; land inequality played a substantial role.
    • Cultural and institutional influences, exemplified by the legacies of the Hanse League and Ottoman Empire, also affected human capital formation.

...

Factors Contributing to Central and Eastern European Industrial Growth (P 57)
...

  • Russian Empire's Initiatives:

    • Government invested heavily in growth-relevant infrastructure, including railroads, banks, and imported technologies.
    • Supported industrial development, sometimes substituting private entrepreneurship.

  • Central and Northern Europe's Innovation:

  • Industry vs. Entrepreneurial Success:

    • Reject the hypothesis of entrepreneurial failure in England for many industries.

  • Welfare Impact:

Dynamics in Agriculture and Services (1870–1913) (P 57-59)
...

Agricultural Sector:
...

  • Employment Changes:
    • Agricultural employment declined, but total production increased due to new fertilizers and improved machinery.
    • Transition from wooden to iron ploughs in poor areas and adoption of threshing machines in richer areas.

European Grain Invasion:
...

  • Transport Cost Decrease:

    • As transport costs decreased, a wave of imported wheat from the Russian Empire and North America flooded Europe.
    • Central Europe responded with the implementation of agricultural tariffs.

  • Impact on Welfare:

    • European grain invasion positively impacted welfare, leading to increased real wages, nutritional levels, and stature improvement during the 1880s.
    • Income redistribution, social security programs, and medical progress supported these trends into the 20th century.

Service Sector Development (1870–1913):
...

  • Agricultural Employment Changes:

    • Agricultural share of employment decreased from just over 50% to just over 40% between 1870 and 1913.
    • Despite industrialization goals, some of the richest countries experienced stronger service sector growth than industrial sector growth.

  • Rich Countries' Service Sector Growth:

    • In the UK, service sector employment increased by 9% (compared to a 2% growth in industry).
    • In Switzerland, service sector employment nearly doubled from 16% to 28%.

  • Urban Expansion and Central Banking:

    • Cities like London, Zürich, and Geneva expanded their central banking and trading positions.

  • Income and Service Sector Employment:

    • Broadberry and O'Rourke (2010) observed a stronger relationship between national income and service sector employment than between income and industrial employment.

...

Migration Patterns:

Land-Labour Ratios:

Decreased Migration Costs:

Emigration Rates:

Factors Influencing Migration:

  • Friends-and-family effect contributed; migrants of certain nationalities increased after initial communities were established.

Migration Selectivity:

  • Brain-drain observed in Russia during the 1890s due to religious persecution among Jewish inhabitants.

Post-World War I and Interwar Period (1914–1945)
...

  • Impact of the First World War:

    • Ended a period of consistent welfare increase in southern, eastern, and central Europe.
    • Unprecedented loss of human life and immense destruction in war areas.
    • War created a climate of distrust and economic disintegration.

  • Era of Deglobalization (1914–1945):

    • Following the first era of globalization (1850–1913), an era of deglobalization occurred (O’Rourke and Williamson 1999).
    • During the war, countries buying European industrial products developed their own industrial capacity.
    • US and Japanese exporters took over markets, meeting Latin American and Asian consumer demands.
    • South-eastern Europe promoted its industrial development, doubling industrial plants with government interference.

  • Unique Development in Eastern Europe:

    • October Revolution in the Russian Empire led to Soviet-style socialism and communism.
    • Nationalization of main industries prioritized heavy industrial production over consumer-oriented light industries and agriculture.
    • Collectivization of agriculture led to harvest failures and a high death toll, especially in Ukraine during the early 1930s.
    • Ambitious expansion of the Soviet educational system and developed heavy industries supported the Soviet Union's success in World War II.

  • Interwar Period in Central Europe:

    • Deglobalization after WWI reached its economic nadir during the Great Depression of the early 1930s.
    • Germany severely affected by the Depression; recovery and rationalization of major industries financed by unsustainable foreign lending.
    • War reparation obligations, reduced investment propensity, and a rigid austerity policy led to deflation.

  • Rise of National Socialists in Germany:

    • Unemployment rates reached unprecedented heights, leading to the rise of the national socialists.
    • Inhumane policies against the Jewish minority, political leftists, and other groups followed.
    • National socialists rapidly abolished democracy, as illustrated in Figure 2.9, depicting the decline of political participation rights in central Europe during the 1930s
      (P 62)

National Socialist Trade Policy in Germany and Great Depression Recovery (P 62)
...

  • National Socialist Trade Policy in Germany:

    • Autarkic policy aimed to eliminate imports, substituting them with domestic alternatives or raw materials.
    • Exempted iron ore and similar items to strengthen military production capacity.
    • Both persecuted and non-persecuted German groups suffered; mortality rates increased, and children's stature growth stagnated.

  • Impact of the Great Depression:

    • Affected all European countries with varying intensity.
    • German reaction included autarkic policies, impacting both persecuted and non-persecuted groups.
    • Mortality rates increased in large cities, and children's stature growth abruptly stagnated.

  • Determinants of Recovery from the Great Depression:

    • Monetary policy played a crucial role.
    • Many countries adopted the gold standard during the nineteenth century.
    • Post-First World War, a return to gold was driven by ideology, providing stability during normal economic periods.
    • Capital mobility during the first era of globalization (1850–1913) benefited from the gold standard.

Impact of the Gold Standard on Recovery and World War II (P 63)
...

  • Gold Standard's Role in Recovery:

    • Eichengreen and Sachs (1985) argued early exit from the gold standard (e.g., UK in 1931) facilitated rapid recovery during the Great Depression.
    • Countries (Italy, Switzerland, France, Belgium, Poland) adhering to gold standard faced reduced competitiveness, hindering recovery.
    • Deflationary processes and agricultural protectionism in the early 1930s exacerbated economic challenges.

  • Soviet Union and Economic Growth:

    • The Soviet Union achieved rapid industrial growth during the Great Depression.
    • Statistics suggest substantial increases, e.g., pig iron production in 1940 was four times that of 1927.
    • Some market economy countries, like Poland, experimented with nationalized industries.

  • Impact of the Second World War:

    • Redirected world leadership to the US and Soviet Union, severely affecting European economies.
    • Nazi army destruction, forced labor, resource plundering, excessive taxes, and raw material 'deliveries' heavily impacted occupied countries.
    • European infrastructure suffered, but German industrial capacity increased despite bombing (mostly specialized in armament).

The golden age of the 1950s and 1960s (P 64)
...

During the two decades following 1950, often referred to as the golden age of European economic growth, several factors contributed to significant economic advancements. Here are key aspects of this period:

  1. Structural Change and Shift from Agriculture to Industry/Services:

    • Countries that had not fully industrialized experienced growth due to a transition from agriculture to industry and services.
    • All European economies became service sector-oriented, with service shares comprising approximately 60%, except for eastern Europe, which had a slightly smaller share.

  2. Market Conditions in Southern and Central Europe:

    • Relatively free markets in southern and central Europe, along with the utilization of labor migration (such as Germany's 'Gastarbeiter' movement), contributed to impressive growth during the 1950s and 1960s.

  3. Formation of European Union (EU) Institutions:

    • The predecessor institutions of the European Union were established during this period, fostering economic integration among the initial set of European countries.

  4. Capital Mobility:

    • Capital shortages, which had been impediments to growth in previous periods, were less significant as capital became more mobile across borders.

  5. Technological Innovations:

    • Marked increases in efficiency were driven by new technologies, particularly from North America. These innovations allowed for input savings, cheaper product provision, and increased demand.

  6. Keynesian Economic Policies:

    • Economic policies during this period were influenced by Keynesian principles, aiming to reduce and smooth business cycle effects. Keynesian policies appeared effective during this era of recovery and stable growth.

Development in Eastern and South-Eastern Europe:
...

  1. Yugoslavia's Unique Growth:

    • According to official statistics, Yugoslavia experienced strong growth, particularly from the 1950s to the early 1980s.
    • The unique socialist system in Yugoslavia, where factories were owned by workers and decision-making was less centralized, may have contributed to stronger growth.

  2. Challenges in Assessing Quality of Growth:

    • Evaluating the quality of physical capital and services in socialist economies was challenging due to factors such as mass production of sometimes poor-quality goods.
    • The breakdown of socialist economies in 1989 led to a reassessment of the value of capital goods in market economies.

  3. Sputnik Shock and Human Capital Development:

    • The Soviet Union's early success in space exploration, exemplified by the launch of the first satellite, Sputnik, indicated rapid advancements in human capital during the first decades of the Soviet Union.

  4. Challenges in Economic Structure Changes:

    • Despite high growth rates, economic incentives and preferences of socialist leadership posed challenges as economic structures changed in subsequent decades.

This period marked a significant phase of economic growth and transformation, characterized by structural shifts, technological advancements, and regional developments in both western and eastern Europe.

Stagflation (1970s–1980s) (P 65)
...

European Economic Dynamics (1970s-1980s)
...

Oil Shock and Stagflation
...

  • After the 1973 oil shock, European growth ceased, leading to stagflation (Crafts and Toniolo, 1996).
  • Stagflation, a combination of inflation and stagnation, defied earlier economic theories.

Energy Prices and Growth
...

  • Energy price surge in the 1970s questioned if resource costs triggered stagflation.
  • Lower energy prices during the 1950s and 1960s benefited European growth (Crafts and Toniolo, 1996).
  • Economic growth slowed due to the inability to sustain North American technology adoption.

Environmental Concerns and Economic Shifts
...

  • 1970s saw rising European skepticism towards unlimited industrial growth.
  • Club of Rome report emphasized unsustainable ecological practices.
  • Green parties emerged, advocating for ecological agendas in various European countries.

Southern Europe's Pursuit of Growth
...

  • Southern European countries prioritized income growth despite environmental concerns.
  • Authoritarian regimes in Portugal and Spain were overthrown, leading to democratization.
  • Portugal and Spain joined the European Community in 1986.

Eastern Europe's Economic Challenges
...

  • Eastern European socialist countries faced unique challenges with energy resources.
  • Selling oil strained political-economic relations within the Eastern bloc after 1973.
  • Soviet Union subsidized its satellite states but struggled with economic inefficiencies.

Gorbachev's Reforms (1986)
...

  • Gorbachev's policy shift in 1986 transformed both political and economic systems.
  • Economic incentives in the socialist system were inadequate, leading to dissatisfaction.
  • Political protests in Poland prompted Gorbachev's significant policy changes in the Soviet Union.

Finale: the 1990s and 2000s (P 66-67)
...

Post-Socialist Economic Transition (1990s-2000s)
...

Harsh Economic Transformation
...

  • Shift from socialist to market economy led to substantial initial GDP decrease.
  • Life expectancy dropped, and crime, including homicide rates, surged in former socialist nations.

Factors Influencing Economic Growth
...

  • Institutional economists migrated to Russia, aiming for rapid economic growth.
  • Entrepreneurial traditions and self-images played significant roles in addition to institutional changes.

European Integration and EU Crisis (1990s-2006)
...

  • Deepening European political-economic integration characterized the 1990s.
  • EU crisis emerged in 2006, impacting member states, including former Soviet republics in the Baltic.

EU Enlargement and Common Currency
...

  • East-central Europe's change enlarged the EU to twenty-eight member states.
  • Introduction of a common currency in many European countries reduced transaction costs and increased mobility.
  • Inflationary tendencies of the 1970s and 1980s disappeared, but challenges arose during financial crises.

Youth Unemployment and Economic Challenges (2000s)
...

  • Youth unemployment surged in Spain, Portugal, Italy, and Greece during the 2000s.
  • Inability to devalue currencies in crises impacted southern European countries' competitiveness.
  • Global economic crisis from 2006 reinforced north–south differences, affecting Germany and southern Europe.

Technological Revolution and Productivity (1990s-2000s)
...

  • Economic restructuring followed the revolution in computer-based information and communication technologies (ICT).
  • Despite ICT's pervasive presence, measured productivity effects in most industries seemed limited.
  • ICT primarily improved workplace conditions and services, resembling the impact of electricity in the early 20th century (Solow, 1987).

Historical Variability in European Economic Development (1500-2000)
...

Early Prosperity and Inequality (c. 1600)
...

  • Southern and eastern Europe showed high living standards around 1600.
  • Urban populations benefited from low grain costs in eastern Europe, ensuring high real wages.
  • Nutritional status was exceptionally high during the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries.

Eastern Europe's Export Impact
...

  • Grain export from eastern to north-western Europe increased food prices.
  • Second serfdom in eastern Europe led to unequal power and income distribution, hindering welfare.

Southern Europe's Historical Highs (14th-15th Centuries)
...

  • Italy, Spain, and Portugal had high human capital and national incomes in the 14th-15th centuries.
  • Central Europe surpassed southern and eastern neighbors in income during the 19th century.
  • Convergence to north-western Europe's income levels was disrupted by World Wars in the early 20th century.

Post-War Prosperity and Setbacks
...

  • Immediate postwar period saw substantial economic growth across all European regions.
  • 1950s and 1960s marked a golden age for many European countries.
  • Socialism in eastern Europe constrained economic development, transforming to market systems in the late 1980s.

Transformation Challenges (Late 1980s-2000s)
...

  • Eastern Europe faced a transformation crisis, transitioning from socialism to market systems.
  • Central Europe struggled for reform, while a global financial crisis impacted the 2000s.
