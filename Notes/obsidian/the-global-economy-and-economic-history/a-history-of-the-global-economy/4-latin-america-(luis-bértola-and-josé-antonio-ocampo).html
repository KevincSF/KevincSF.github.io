Diverse Landscape of Latin America (P 121)
...

  • Challenges in Regional Analysis
    • Latin America encompasses diverse countries with varying characteristics.
    • Common historical features like Iberian colonial experience and reliance on natural resources provide a unit of analysis.
  • Notion of the 'Developed World'
    • Latin America is not considered part of the 'developed world.'
    • Disparities in living standards persist, with some countries facing significant poverty and developmental challenges.

Transformation and Challenges
...

  • Strides in Development
    • Latin America has undergone notable economic, social, and political changes.
    • Achieved middle-income status globally, marking significant progress.
  • Persistent Patterns
    • Natural resource-based production patterns remain prevalent.
    • Trade specialization hinders access to technologically dynamic global market segments, impeding growth.
  • Cyclical Access to Capital Markets
    • Latin America faces cyclical challenges in accessing capital markets.
    • Such fluctuations undermine sustained development efforts in the region.

Contrasting Development Paths
...

  • Leveraging Natural Resource Endowments
    • Some regions, termed 'Western offshoots' (U.S., Canada, Australia, New Zealand), leverage natural resources for successful development.
    • European Nordic countries also demonstrate successful utilization of natural resources.
  • East Asian Success Story
    • East Asian countries with abundant labor supply surpass Latin America in sustained economic growth.
    • Development strategies based on labor supply contribute to improved quality of life.

Ongoing Challenges and Global Comparisons
...

  • Persisting Challenges in Latin America
    • Despite strides, Latin American countries struggle to break free from natural resource dependence.
    • Trade limitations and cyclical capital market access impede holistic development.
  • Global Contrasts in Development Paths
    • Contrasts between Latin America and successful regions highlight the impact of resource utilization.
    • Learning from success stories can inform strategies for overcoming persistent challenges.

Challenges in Latin American Development (P 122)
...

  • Limited Economic Success
    • Struggles in economic development hinder sustained welfare policies.
    • Complex factors impact the region's ability to make radical societal and economic changes.
  • Multifaceted Determinants of Economic Performance
    • Economic analysis alone cannot unveil the barriers to development.
    • Social, cultural, political relationships, and geographical factors intricately shape economic outcomes.

Institutional Development and Historical Context
...

  • Debate on Institutional Role
    • Ongoing debate on the role of institutions in development theory.
    • Social structures, power distribution, elite influence, and weak nation-states are crucial factors.
  • Legacy of Colonial Times
    • Historical factors, including colonial legacy and post-independence challenges, shape Latin America's successes and failures.
    • Economic and political difficulties post-independence influence global positioning.

Analyzing Latin America's Development Phases
...

  • State-Building (1820–70)

    • Painful state-building phase shapes political landscape.
    • Challenges in creating robust nation-states contribute to long-term weaknesses.

  • Export-Led Growth (1870–1929)

    • Period of economic expansion through export-led growth.
    • Historical context influences economic trajectory.

  • Twentieth Century Shifts (1980 Onward)

    • State-led industrialization until 1980, followed by a return to market-oriented approaches.
    • Changing economic strategies impact development outcomes.

Analyzing Obstacles to Development
...

  • Volatility of Commodity Prices

    • Commodity price fluctuations create economic instability.
    • Impact on sustained development efforts.

  • Trade Balance Issues

    • Trade imbalances hinder economic growth.
    • Addressing trade-related challenges is crucial for sustained development.

  • Education and Inequality

    • Educational disparities and income inequality impact development.
    • Addressing these issues is essential for broad-based improvements in quality of life.

Pursuing Industrial and Technology Policy
...

  • Avoiding Previous Obstacles
    • Concluding with insights on Latin America's industrial and technology policy.
    • Addressing historical obstacles crucial for sustainable growth and development.

Latin America in the world economy: convergence and divergence in per capita GDP (P 123)
...

GDP Trends and Historical Phases in Latin America
...

  • Limited Historical GDP Data
    • Historical GDP data for Latin America, especially in the nineteenth century, is limited.
    • Per capita GDP trends reveal fluctuations around the global average over two centuries.
  • Three Major Phases
    • Phase 1 (Independence - 1870): Relative decline compared to Western leaders.
    • Phase 2 (1870–1980): Upward trend in per capita GDP.
    • Phase 3 (1980 Onward): Decline in per capita GDP since the 1980s.
  • Comparison with Other Continents
    • Outpacing Africa in economic growth, maintaining superiority.
    • Outdistanced Asia until mid-twentieth century; reversed since 1980.

Concepts of Divergence
...

  • Small Divergence vs. Great Divergence
    • Small Divergence: Slow growth in the 'rest of the world' (1500–1820), driven by population.
    • Great Divergence (1820 Onward): Western per capita GDP growth surpasses the 'rest of the world'.
    • Latin America mirrors the 'rest of the world' growth pattern post-Independence.

Growth Patterns and West-Latin America Gap
...

  • Similar Growth Patterns
    • Latin America post-Independence follows the growth pattern of the 'rest of the world.'
    • Population growth contributes to around 60% of economic growth.
  • Per Capita GDP Gap
    • Annual growth rates of Latin America's per capita GDP 75% of the West's rates.
    • Gap widens from 0.8 to 2.7 times Latin America's per capita GDP between 1820 and 2008.
  • Colonial Period
    • Maddison's assumptions suggest a substantial gap during the colonial period.
    • Standards of living declined initially but recovered, leading to a relatively stable gap by the end of colonization.

...

  • Initial Gap Stability

Early Independence Era (c. 1820–1870): Economic Growth and Challenges (P 128)
...

  • Debates on Economic Growth:
    • Ongoing debate among historians on whether economic growth occurred during the first half century of Independence (c. 1820–1870) in Latin America.
    • Some argue for positive developments, while others interpret the era as marked by delays in development due to conflicts and political instability.
  • Aggregate Economic Indicators (c. 1820–1870):
    • Per Capita GDP Growth:
      • Grew at a modest annual rate of 0.2%.
    • Per Capita Exports:
      • Exhibited stronger growth at around 2%.
    • Per Capita Output for Domestic Market:
      • Virtually flat.
  • Regional Differences in Growth Rates:
    • Group 3 (Southern Cone Countries):
      • Realized the greatest increase in exports and performed better in per capita GDP.
    • Group 1 (Indo-European Societies):
      • Experienced variations in per capita GDP growth rates.
    • Group 2 (Afro-American Societies - Tropical Economies):
      • Witnessed the worst development around 1820, relying on slave labor.
  • Comparative Per Capita GDP (1870):
    • Group 3's per capita GDP more than doubled Group 1's, with significant growth.
    • Tropical economies (Group 2) grew more swiftly, surpassing Group 1 by 1870.
  • Historical Context:
    - Wars of independence were concluding or ongoing in 1820, impacting economic conditions.
    - Group-specific variations in performance with notable transformations by 1870.

Challenges and Anthropometric Data (c. 1820–1870): A Closer Look (P 131)
...

  • Welfare Indicators and Anthropometric Data:
    • Slow growth and potential stagnation in the decades following Independence are reinforced by anthropometric data.
    • Average Male Stature:
      • Argentina and Peru saw a decline in average male stature by the 1870s compared to the eve of the colonial period.
      • Brazil witnessed a stagnating trend initially, slightly reverting after the 1850s.
  • Country Examples (1820–70 Development Phase):
    • Peru (Group 1):
      • Example of Group 1 with characteristics of central areas of the colonial economy.
      • Population more than two and a half times larger than Argentina in 1820.
      • Large silver mining sector and a concentrated campesino-based sector in subsistence agriculture.
      • War of Independence was a bloody struggle, impacting the economy.
      • Output averaged 87% of 1800 levels during 1830–70 due to the collapse of silver mines.
      • Exports remained flat until the early 1840s before the guano boom, despite diversification into cotton, wool, and saltpetre.
  • Context of Struggles and Economic Impact:
    • War of Independence in Peru involved reprisals against royalists and supporting local elites.
    • Economic hardships from the collapse of silver mines impacted output and exports.
    • The guano boom in the early 1840s provided some relief but followed a challenging period.

Impact of Silver Decline and Hacienda Self-Reliance (Group 1) (P 132)
...

  • Decline of silver-based economy led to reduced production in haciendas.
  • Haciendas turned self-reliant due to radical protectionism (Gootenberg 1989).

Coastal Export Activities and Slave-Labor Dissolution (Group 1)
...

  • Coastal export activities affected by dissolution of slave-labor system (Gootenberg 1989).
  • Haciendas became self-reliant due to economic challenges.

Guano Trade Boom and Economic Impact in Peru (Group 1) (P 133)
...

  • Guano trade boom interrupted economic decline in Peru.
  • Exports surged sevenfold from 1845 to 1860, with guano contributing over 50% (Contreras and Cueto 2004: 116).

Mexico's Turbulent Period and Recovery (Group 1)
...

  • Short recovery in Mexico post-Independence disrupted by civil wars (Sánchez Santiró 2009b).
  • Porfirio Díaz's government paved the way for economic growth amid institutional instability.

Conflicts and Deepening Divisions in Mexico (Group 1)
...

  • Conflicts in mid-1850s affected vast rural areas, involving caste clashes.
  • Deepened political and ideological divisions between liberals, conservatives, republicans, and monarchists (Sánchez Santiró 2009a: 102–03).

Brazil's Economic Stagnation and Regional Diversity (Group 2) (P 134)
...

  • Brazil faced stagnation and regional diversity post-independence (1822–72).
  • GDP growth failed to outpace population growth, resulting in frustration until the 20th century (Leff 1982, 1997).

North-Eastern Brazil: Decline in Sugar and Cotton Exports (Group 2)
...

  • North-east, with sugar and cotton exports, declined; Dutch disease cited by Leff (1982, 1997).
  • Coffee exports in the south-east rose from 26% to 47% of total exports (Mitchell 2003).

Factors Contributing to North-East's Decline (Group 2)
...

  • Dutch disease led to a decline in the north-east due to rising importance of coffee exports.
  • Restructuring sugar industry faced challenges; large-scale inter-regional migration difficult.

Brazil's Export Sector Dynamics and Low Per Capita Income (Group 2)
...

  • Brazil had a pattern of high export earnings coexisting with low per capita income.
  • Coffee industry expansion unhindered by labor cost increase, with subsidized immigration flows (Leff 1997: 35).

Colombia's Economic Contraction and Period of Stagnation (Group 2)
...

  • Colombia experienced economic contraction post-independence, followed by stagnation until around 1850 (Ocampo 1984, 1990).
  • Collapse of gold production along the Pacific coast and crisis in Cartagena and Santander.

Colombia's Growth Spurt and Export Diversification (Group 2)
...

  • Growth spurt from mid-19th century to early 1880s fueled by export diversification.
  • Boom-bust cycles in new exports like tobacco, cotton, indigo, cinchona bark, and coffee (Kalmanovitz and López Rivera 2009).

Antioquia's Early Economic Recovery and Gold Production (Group 2) (P 134-135)
...

  • Antioquia in Colombia experienced early economic recovery post-Independence.
  • Gold production continued, becoming a more modern business venture with improved terms of trade.

Challenges to Growth in Early Years of Independence (P 136)
...

  • Nation-State Building Challenges:
    • Difficulty in building and consolidating new nation-states.
    • Weakness of local elites, a colonial legacy, hindered state-building efforts.
  • Resistance to Liberal Reforms:
    • Severe obstacles against the introduction of liberal reforms.
    • Regions where elites deemed slavery economically necessary resisted liberal reforms.

Impact on Latin American Development (1820–70)
...

  • Institutional Change and Republic Formation:
    • New freedom allowed rapid institutional change as Latin American countries became republics.
    • Rapid population growth, especially in Group 3 (Southern Cone countries like Argentina).
  • Economic Disparities in Development:
    • Argentina exemplifies Group 3 but faced disparities with strong decline in some regions.
    • Mixed economic development: decline in the north-west, expansion in Buenos Aires region (Bértola and Ocampo 2012).

Welfare and Income Development Uncertainty
...

  • Ambiguous Income Growth and Welfare:
    • Unclear if 1820–70 brought modest income growth or improved welfare.
    • Latin America fell behind Europe and North America, per capita GDP gap swelling from 0.8 to 1.8 times (1820–1870).

Latin America's Economic Growth and Inequality Trends (P 136)
...

19th Century Export-Led Growth
...

  • Latin America experienced rapid growth, paralleling developed nations (Gelman, 2011).
  • Export expansion, notably in Argentina pre-First World War, drove economic growth.

Population Dynamics and Migration
...

  • Uneven population growth, with Group 3 countries leading until 1929.
  • European and Asian migration, particularly to Argentina, Brazil, Cuba, and Peru.

Export Expansion and World Trade (p 137)
...

  • Latin American exports surged annually by 4.2% between 1870-1929 (Gelman, 2011).
  • Varied export performances across countries, with Group 3 leading until 1913.

Rising Inequality and Convergence
...

  • Inequality increased post-independence, but Latin America's GDP per capita converged.
  • Southern Cone economies slowed, while others performed relatively well till the 1950s.

Transition to Industrialization (P 138)
...

  • High income reliant on commodity exports, problematic during 1930s-40s deglobalization.
  • Latin America shifted towards state-led industrialization post-deglobalization period.

How did Latin America develop during the twentieth century relative to the rest of the world? (P 138)
...

1870-1980: Relative Improvement
...

  • Latin America improved its global ranking while 'rest of the world' declined.
  • Latin America's share of world output rose steadily from 2.6% (1870) to 9.5% (1980).

Gap with the West
...

  • Despite gains, Latin America couldn't narrow the gap with the West.
  • Western economies widened the gap during 1950-1973 'golden age' of capitalism.

Post-1980 Challenges
...

  • Latin America fell further behind developed economies since 1980.
  • Lost ground relative to world average, with slower growth compared to Asia.

Analysis: Convergence/Divergence Cycles, Financial Crises, and Volatility (P 138)
...

Factors Driving Convergence and Divergence
...

  • Periods of growth reduce income gap with developed countries but don't sustain convergence.
  • High volatility of growth rates across Latin American countries exacerbates trends.

Excessive Volatility in Latin American Economies (P 139)
...

  • Latin American economies display significantly higher volatility than global and OECD averages.
  • Volatility impacts social, corporate, institutional, and political stability, hindering long-term investments.

Procyclical Fiscal and Macroeconomic Policies
...

  • Procyclical policies worsen shocks, undermining production activity (Kaminsky et al., 2004; Ocampo and Vos, 2008).
  • Policy exacerbation rather than dampening further shocks is observed.

Dependency on Natural Resources
...

  • Reliance on natural resources exposes Latin America to price fluctuations and demand shifts.
  • Production structures concentrated in few commodities increase vulnerability to market changes.

Impact of International Capital Flows
...

  • Procyclical capital flows amplify economic volatility in developing countries (Triffin, 1968; Ocampo, 2008).
  • Positive growth effects are magnified by capital inflows, while downturns exacerbate slumps.

Historical Patterns and Trends
...

  • Volatility remained high since the interwar period, with no clear correlation with income levels.
  • No discernible downward trend observed in volatility levels since the twentieth century.

Financial Crises: Frequency and Severity
...

Historical Patterns
...
  • Peaks in financial crises follow periods of hefty capital inflows.
  • Booms in external financing tied to international business cycles precede crises.
Major International Financial Booms
...
  • Booms preceding the First World War and the 2008–09 recession didn't result in crises.
  • However, both led to regional recessions following the boom periods.
Impact on Latin America
...
  • Crises, including those in 1873, the 1930s, 1980s debt crisis, and post-1997 crises, affected almost all Latin American countries.
  • Recent crises merged into a prolonged, widespread crisis across the region.

Figure 4.2 (P 141)

Dominant Crisis Types
...

  • Debt crises prevalent since Independence in Latin America.
  • Steep devaluations from balance-of-payments crises common since the First World War.

Evolution of Crisis Types
...

  • Mid-1950s to mid-1960s saw crises without preceding external financing booms.
  • Banking sector crises increasingly frequent since the 1980s.

Dual and Triple Crises
...

  • Since the 1930s, most crises have been dual (debt and balance-of-payments) in nature.
  • Since the 1980s, many crises are triple (including banking crises).

Additional Dimensions
...

  • High inflation, often correlated with balance-of-payments crises.
  • Occasional domestic debt crises add to the complexity.

Triggering Factors
...

  • Crises typically initiated by sudden export collapses during critical international conjunctures.
  • Plummeting commodity prices lead to trade deficits coinciding with reduced external financing.

Integration into the World Economy: Volatility of Commodity Prices (P 142)
...

External Factors Driving Volatility
...

  • Fluctuations in external markets or terms of trade contribute to volatility.
  • Latin America experiences more volatility compared to its 'relevant world'.

Impact of Terms of Trade
...

  • Terms of trade transmit greater volatility to Latin America.
  • Close correlation between export product concentration and volatility (correlation coefficient: 0.44).

Product Specialization Debate
...

  • Latin America's extreme concentration in a few product categories affects volatility.
  • Long-standing structuralist perspective sees specialization hindering rapid growth.

Importance of Diversification
...

  • Fastest-growing economies diversified production, focusing on high-technology manufacturing exports.
  • Growth trends highlight the significance of diversification for development (Hausmann et al., 2007; Ocampo et al., 2009).

Waves of Globalization and Latin America's Trade Position (P 144)
...

First Wave of Globalization (Pre-1914)
...
  • Based on raw materials and food products exchanged for manufactures.
  • Latin America benefited due to its specialization pattern.
Collapse of International Division of Labour (1914–1945)
...
  • Shift to intra-industry trade among developed countries post-World War II.
  • Strong protectionism against developing world's agricultural and textile products.
Decline in Latin America's Trade Share
...
  • Latin America's share of world trade dropped below 1920s levels due to protectionism and industrialization biases.
  • Second wave of globalization in the 1960s offered export opportunities, leading to partial recovery.
Limited Recovery in Trade Position
...
  • Latin America regained some lost ground with policy shifts towards export growth.
  • However, still far from reaching trade levels seen during the first wave of globalization.

Trends in the Terms of Trade (P 144)
...

Pre-First World War Boom
...

  • Real prices of agricultural and mineral products rose, especially before WWI.
  • Upward trend reversed in stages post-WWI, with intermittent downward phases.

Significant Reductions in Prices
...

  • Strong reduction in the 1920s and another in the 1980s-1990s.
  • Agricultural goods prices dropped sharply, while mining products declined moderately.

Impact on Commodities Terms of Trade
...

  • Commodities (excluding oil) terms of trade fell by 60% from pre-WWI to 1998–2003.
  • Tropical agricultural products experienced the most significant decline.

Oil Prices Dynamics
...

  • Real oil prices also fell, with a later decrease compared to non-oil commodities.
  • Oil prices maintained substantial gains from 1970s oil shocks despite the 1980s plunge.

Potential Return to Pre-Globalization Patterns
...

  • 2004 commodity price boom, driven by Chinese demand, resembles first wave of globalization.
  • Raises questions about returning to historical trade patterns.

Prebisch-Singer Thesis Relevance
...

  • Long-term downturn in tropical goods prices prompts reconsideration of Prebisch-Singer thesis.
  • Highlights structural and institutional differences between manufacturing and tropical goods sectors.

  • Brazilian and Caribbean laborers faced competition from low-wage tropical countries.

Adjusted Prebisch-Singer Thesis (P 146)
...

  • Worldwide demand shifts favored higher-quality products, posing challenges for Latin America.
  • Industrialization in Europe and North America based on high-demand products for rising incomes.

Income Elasticity of Demand
...

  • Until WWI, rising incomes in Europe and the US favored Latin American products.
  • Meat and wheat remained highly demanded, showing high income elasticity.
  • After dietary transition, demand curve displayed low income elasticity, reducing demand for Latin American commodities during periods of income growth.

Trade Balance and Balance of Payments Challenges (P 146)
...

Impact of Demand Disparities
...

  • Higher demand for foreign products without proportional demand for Latin American products leads to balance of payments difficulties.
  • Structural changes required to produce goods with higher income elasticity for foreign markets.

ECLAC's Theory
...

  • Commodity exporters face higher income elasticity of import demand compared to export demand.
  • Insufficient structural change leads to long-standing trade deficits.

Growth Determinants
...

  • Growth rate linked to relative propensities to export and import.
  • Income elasticity of demand for exports and imports crucial for long-term growth.

Long-Term Growth Estimates (P 147)
...

  • Latin America's per capita GDP relative to the West dropped from 36% (1870) to 27% (2008).
  • Relative income elasticity factor explains drop to 31%, with remaining 4% attributed to other factors like terms of trade decline or over-indebtedness.

Education and Development in Latin America (P 147)
...

Comparative Educational Disadvantage
...

  • Latin America's education efforts lag behind, hindering convergence with other regions.
  • Population completed average of 7.1 years of schooling around 2000, compared to 12.5 in dominant world countries.

Progress in the Twentieth Century
...

  • Significant educational progress made during the twentieth century.
  • Population averaged just 1.5 years of schooling at the start of the century.

Relationship Between Education and GDP per Capita
...

  • Higher educational levels associated with higher GDP per capita values.
  • Latin America's education level lower than other regions for any given income level.

Historical Context of Education System
...

  • Colonial education system aimed to preserve existing social order.
  • Modern education system emerged with the creation of independent states, alongside political and economic changes.
  • Progress in education system marred by division between advocates of democratic, inclusive education and proponents of hierarchical, authoritarian structures.

Inequality (P 148)
...

Historical Perspectives on Inequality (P 149)
...

  • Oligarchic aspects of Latin American development led to wealth and power concentration.
  • First wave of globalization reinforced elites' property rights and political power.

Neo-Institutional Theories
...

  • Colonial institutions created long-term inequalities, hindered human capital formation, and slowed growth.
  • Coatsworth (2008) argues economic power concentration fostered development, contrary to neo-institutionalists.

State-led Industrialization and Inequality (P 150)
...

  • Welfare-state development during state-led industrialization led to declining inequality in Argentina, Chile, and Uruguay.
  • Industrialization exacerbated inequality in countries like Brazil but improved distribution in Mexico, Venezuela, and Colombia.

Late Twentieth Century Market Reforms
...

  • Market reforms in late twentieth century increased inequality via deregulation, state capacity destruction, and deindustrialization.
  • Economic crises and restructuring favored skilled labor, worsening income distribution.
  • Distribution improved in the early twenty-first century due to educational opportunities and social assistance policies.

Present challenges from a historical perspective (P 151)
...

1. Macroeconomic Management
...

  • Consolidate achievements in inflation and fiscal sustainability.
  • Address vulnerability to external shocks, especially cyclical currency appreciations and boom-bust cycles.

2. Economic Growth
...

  • High growth rates require proactive production and technology policies, not just sound macroeconomic fundamentals.
  • Enhance education quality and curriculum alignment with production needs to change regional production patterns.

3. Institution-Building (P 151-152)
...

  • Overcome reliance on natural resource rents and special relationships with the state through education and technology development.
  • Seek an appropriate mix of state and market involvement, focusing on developing state capabilities.

4. Social Debt and Equity Agenda (P 152)
...

  • Address the historical legacy of extreme economic and social inequality.
  • Combine social policy with economic reforms to tackle income distribution disparities.
  • Focus on social cohesion and equity alongside changes in education, technology, and production to integrate Latin America into the global economy more dynamically.
