Introduction

Common Features of Middle Eastern Development (Issawi, 1982)
...

  • Heterogeneous Development:
    • Spanning from Morocco to Afghanistan, including former Soviet Republics with significant Muslim populations.
  • European Influence:
    • Nineteenth-century contact posed a significant challenge.
    • European merchants dominated non-agricultural sectors pre-World War I.
  • Twentieth-century Reaction:
    • Middle Eastern leaders aimed to reduce European influence.
    • Efforts to lessen the economic dominance of religious minorities.

Quantitative Analysis of Economic Trends (Pamuk, 2012)
...

  • Urban Real Wages:
    • Şevket Pamuk provided estimates for various Middle Eastern countries.
  • Early Modern Inequality (Coşgel and Ergene, 2012):
    • Analyzed inequality using tax registers for northern Anatolia.
  • Biological Standard of Living:
    • Studied for Middle Eastern economies, showing relative advantage over Europe in the mid-19th century.
    • Significant decline since the 1880s, impacting welfare perceptions.

Education and Human Capital (Stegl and Baten, 2009)
...

  • Historical Education Development:
    • Flourished during the High Middle Ages but declined thereafter.
  • Underinvestment:
    • Governments and families underinvested in education, hindering economic growth.
  • Substantial Differences:
    • Notable variations among Middle Eastern countries warrant study.

Medieval and early modern period (P 209)
...

Technological Superiority and Knowledge Flow (P 209)
...

Medieval Technological Advancement (Bosker et al., 2013)
...

  • Superiority of Middle Eastern Technology:
    • Middle Eastern technology surpassed Europe during the medieval period.
    • Knowledge exchange favored the flow from the Middle East to Europe.
  • Medical Knowledge:
    • Middle Eastern physicians not only preserved ancient knowledge but also advanced it.
  • Urbanization Rates:
    • Bosker et al. (2013) compared urbanization levels between the Middle East and Europe.
    • Urbanization in the Middle East outpaced Europe until roughly 1100.
    • Middle Eastern urbanization plateaued after 1100 and even declined during invasions and plague.
    • European urbanization increased steadily, surpassing the Middle East by 1800.

Formation of Middle Eastern Empires (P 210)
...

Expansion of Arabic and Ottoman Empires
...

  • Arabic Empires:
    • Emerged in the early medieval period, marking the beginning of large empires in the Middle East.
  • Ottoman Expansion:
    • Fifteenth-century expansion towards the Balkans and south-eastern Europe.
    • Sixteenth-century expansion towards eastern territories and north Africa.
    • Seventeenth-century dominance from Bosnia to Iraq and the Arabian Peninsula.
    • Morocco remained the sole independent power in north Africa.

Economic History of the Middle East (Findlay and O’Rourke, 2007)
...

  • Speculation of Decline:
    • Limited quantitative evidence during the early modern period.
    • Speculation arose due to perceptions of backward technology and poverty in the eighteenth century.
  • Absolute vs. Relative Decline:
    • Unclear whether it was absolute decline or relative to north-western Europe's enormous development.
  • Shift in Trade Routes:
    • Major event in Middle Eastern economic history.
    • Middle East lost monopoly profits from trade routes shifting towards Europe.
    • Trade of Yemenite coffee provided an alternative source of income for Middle Eastern merchants (Raymond, 1973, cited from Owen, 1993).

Impact of Trade Route Shift
...

  • Monopoly Profits Shift:
    • From Middle East to European powers like Portugal, the Dutch, British, and French.
  • Limited Influence on Middle Eastern Economies:
    • Trading income affects only a small part of the population.

Effects of Plague Events and Mongol Invasion (Borsch, 2005)
...

  • Impact on Irrigation Economies:
    • Particularly severe in Iraq and Egypt due to reliance on irrigation systems.
    • Population density drop hindered maintenance of irrigation systems.
  • Mamluk Rule in Egypt (Findlay and O’Rourke, 2007):
    • Reduced investment in public works necessary for irrigation.
    • Plague-induced population decline lowered peasant numbers and rents.

Real Wage Developments (Pamuk and Ozmucur)
...

  • Solid Evidence on Urban Craftsmen Wages:
    • Similar to southern and central Europe during the sixteenth to eighteenth centuries.
    • Middle Eastern urban centers maintained similar welfare levels compared to other European cities.
  • Specific Social Group Focus:
    • Estimates may not reflect the development of all societal groups.
    • Indicates plausible support for Middle Eastern living standards equal to Europe until the eighteenth century.

Human Capital Formation (Juif and Baten, 2013; Baten and Ghanem, 2014)
...

  • Limited Participation in European Human Capital Revolution:
    • Sixteenth-century Maghreb had only 10% basic numeracy, reaching 50% by the early eighteenth century.
    • Eighteenth-century Turkey and Syria estimates range from 10% to 50% numeracy.
  • European Competitiveness:
    • European numeracy grew from approximately 50% to 90% in the same period, emerging as a strong competitor.

Limitations to the ‘rule of law’ at the beginning of the nineteenth century (P 213-214)
...

Institutional Complexity and Lack of Rule of Law (Kuran, 2011)
...

  • Complicated Institutional Structure:
    • Rule of law was often absent.
    • Taxation capability was low; rulers were often exploitative.
    • Ethnic differences between rulers and ruled were common in places like Iran and Egypt.

Benefits of Low Rule of Law: Maghreb Pirates
...

  • Pirate Economy in Algeria and Tunisia:
    • Originated from holy war traditions against Christians.
    • Developed into a lucrative industry capturing ships and selling captives as slaves.
    • Well-organized business involving ship owners, captains, naval crew, armed warriors, and former hostages.

Property Rights and Land Tenure
...

  • Importance of Clear Property Rights in Irrigation Economies:
    • Property rights were not always clearly defined in Egypt and Mesopotamia.
  • Complex Land Tenure System (Owen, 1993):
    • State ownership of most land except for specific areas like gardens and orchards.
    • Complicated land rights with communal redistribution in some regions.
    • Trend towards imitating private ownership emerged in mid-nineteenth century.

Taxation and Infrastructure
...

  • Taxation and Tax Evasion:
    • Tax rates varied; evasion was common due to perceived lack of public goods.
  • Poor Infrastructure:
    • Roads and infrastructure were relatively undeveloped.
    • Irrigation systems lacked central oversight.

Iran's Historical Challenges
...

  • Invasions and Nomadic Rulers:
    • Suffered during the late Middle Ages and early modern period.
    • Nomadic rulers had difficulty in raising regular taxes, leading to arbitrary confiscations.
  • Impact on Land Use:
    • Relatively insecure property rights and government preference for nomadic tribes led to reduction of irrigated land and increase in pastures and wasteland before 1800.

Industrial Landscape in the Middle East (p 214)
...

Concentration of Industrial Production in Urban Centers
...

  • Urban Concentration:
    • Unlike western European proto-industrial production in rural areas, Middle Eastern industrial production was primarily urban-based.
  • Main Functions of Urban Centers:
    • Trade in suqs (markets) and administration were predominant alongside industrial production.
  • Geographical Setting of Cities (Owen, 1993):
    • Cities were situated near cultivatable land with reasonable soil quality.
  • Key Industries:
    • Textile production was predominant, supplemented by food processing, furniture, and specialized industries.
  • Quality of Production (Owen, 1993):
    • Technological progress in production was slower compared to Europe.
    • Exceptions in industries like Turkish armament production and shipbuilding matched European quality due to military interests.

Constraints on Innovation
...

  • Guild Systems:
    • Fixed price systems and guild structures hindered innovation.
    • Old masters typically commanded younger apprentices, preserving craftsmanship but limiting innovation.

Caravan Trade Organization (Owen, 1993)
...

  • Main Caravan Routes:
    • Connected Syria and Mesopotamia.
    • Extended to Persia and sometimes China.
  • Trade Goods:
    • Textiles, spices from India.
    • European manufactures, African ivory.
    • Middle Eastern goods like cotton thread.
  • Slavery:
    • Black slaves northward, white slaves southward.
  • Ottoman Empire Trade:
    • Grain, sugar, cotton from Egypt.
    • Textiles, soap, dyestuffs from Syria.
  • Transit Trade:
    • Limited Mesopotamian imports/exports.
    • Persia exported opium, carpets.
  • Transport Costs:
    • Bedouin "dues" up to three times normal cost.

Economic Situation (Owen, 1993)
...

  • Property Rights:
    • Complex and varied.
  • Economic Dynamism:
    • Generally low but with exceptions.
  • Production and Taxation:
    • Did not encourage development.
  • Land Resources:
    • Not fully utilized.

Development Paths in the Middle East during the Nineteenth Century
...

Tanzimat Reforms in the Ottoman Empire (P 216)
...

  • Initiation and Scope: (Coşgel and Ergene 2012)
    • Tanzimat reforms (1839) impacted law, administration, military, and economy.
  • Equality and Citizenship:
    • Jews, Orthodox, and Armenian Christians had distinct status pre-reform.
    • Reforms aimed for equality among all citizens.
  • Military Service Regulation:
    • Limited military service to a maximum of five years.
  • Abolition of Tax Farming:
    • Previously, tax farming led to overtaxing and conflicts.
    • Reforms successively abolished this problematic economic institution.
  • Economic Opening:
    • Reforms opened Ottoman economy to imports.
    • Influence from European governments highlighted industrial competition.

Forced Development in Egypt
...

  • Underlying Dynamics: (Coşgel and Ergene 2012)
    • Egypt experienced forced development under Ottoman rule.
  • Economic and Social Changes:
    • Ottoman control led to significant economic and social transformations.
  • Impact of External Influences:
    • European interventions shaped Egyptian development trajectory.

Direct Colonization in Central Asia and Algeria
...

  • Colonial Imposition: (Coşgel and Ergene 2012)
    • Central Asia and Algeria directly colonized during this period.
  • Political and Economic Domination:
    • Colonizers exerted control over governance and economy.
  • Resistance and Consequences:
    • Indigenous populations resisted colonization efforts.
    • Colonial policies resulted in long-term social and economic ramifications.

Egypt's Development under Mohammad Ali Pasha (P 217)
...

  • Reforms and Industrialization

    • Initiated radical reforms in state and economy.
    • Aimed to build a strong army and modernize education.
    • Established factories for ships, chemicals, textiles, and more.
    • Controlled imports/exports to boost government revenue.

  • Agricultural Focus

    • Reformed land ownership, irrigation, and crops.
    • Increased agricultural production substantially.

  • Military Expansion

    • Implemented mandatory military service.
    • Expanded army, engaging in various wars.

  • Transition and Decline under Ismail Pasha

    • Focused on science, agriculture, and abolished slavery.
    • Flourishing cotton production in the 1860s.
    • Faced disasters, foreign debt, and weakened economy.
    • Increased interventionist policies by colonial powers.

Mohammad Ali's reign marked a transformative era in Egypt's history, with significant reforms and industrial advancements, albeit followed by economic challenges and colonial intervention under his successors.

Colonization of Central Asia by Russia (P 218)
...

  • Territorial Expansion

    • Russia expanded into northern steppe of central Asia in mid-18th century.
    • Initial Kazakh interpretation as temporary alliance under Russian supremacy.
    • Russian Empire integrated northern steppe during early 19th century.

  • Control and Integration

    • Three main Kazakh leaders suspended between 1822 and 1848.
    • Russian forts constructed to control conquered territories.
    • Russian settlers provided land, reducing space for nomadic tribes.
    • Forced sedentary lifestyle adoption for many nomads.
    • Immigrant population ranged from 5 to 15 percent in various regions.

  • Conflicts and Rebellions

    • Many conflicts between 1820s and 1840s, with attacks on Slavic settlements.
    • Russian troops suppressed rebellions in 1846.

  • Numeracy and Human Capital

    • Kazakhs were numerically proficient during early 19th century.
    • Russia experienced human capital revolution, surpassing Kazakhs.
    • Kazakh numeracy remained higher than more urbanized Central Asians.

Factors Influencing Early Numeracy Levels in Central Asia (P 219)
...

Settler Share and Contact Learning (Prayon and Baten 2013)
...

  • Settler share partly explains early numeracy.
    • Russians were minority but influential.
  • Good nutritional situation likely contributed.
    • Absence of protein malnutrition.
  • Russian settlers stimulated "contact learning."
    • Kazakhs observed and adopted similar human capital investment.

Regional Characteristics and Historical Events
...

  • Northern Steppe vs. Southern Central Asia
    • Northern: Low population density (1-2/km²).
    • Southern: Higher density, urbanization rate up to 15-20%.
  • Silk Road Region
    • Ancient trade route, high urban development.
    • Experienced various rulers: Mongols, Persians, Arabs.
    • Russian invasion later than northern steppe, motivations included economic interests and geopolitical concerns.
  • Uzbekistan and Samarkand
    • Most numerate region but faced challenges.
    • Numeracy stagnated before Russian conquest.
    • Dramatic fall during colonization in the 1860s.
    • Modest recovery followed but still below Russian levels.

Numeracy Trends in Algeria and Colonial Impacts (P 220)
...

Initial High Numeracy and Stagnation (Baten and Ghanem 2014)
...

  • Numeracy initially high but stagnated until 1870s.

Indigenous Work Ethic and Colonial Conflicts (Deutsches Staats-Wörterbuch 1857)
...

  • Indigenous farmers described as industrious and hard-working.
  • Heavy and prolonged conflicts between French and indigenous populations.

European Settlement and Assimilation Efforts
...

  • European Settlement
    • Algeria heavily settled by Europeans, nearly a fifth of the population.
  • Assimilation Efforts
    • French aimed to assimilate Algeria into France.
    • Substantial educational investments made, especially post-1900.

Indigenous Resistance and Cultural Preservation
...

  • Indigenous resistance against assimilation.
  • Maintained individual skills and relatively human-capital-intensive agriculture.

Summary of Colonial Impacts
...

  • Three Types of Fundamental Change
    1. Tanzimat reforms opening economies to European competition.
    2. Mohammad Ali's infant-industry strategy in Egypt.
    3. Direct colonization, leading to relative decline in human capital in Silk Road region and stagnation in Algeria.

Why did the Middle East deindustrialize during the nineteenth century? (P 220-222)
...

Trade-Based Interpretation (Pamuk and Williamson 2010)
...

  • Ottoman Empire self-sufficient in textiles until 18th century.
    • Textile market dominance declined drastically by 1910.
  • Imported British textiles outperformed local production.
  • Shift to cash crops due to improved terms of trade.
  • Despite deindustrialization, real incomes grew slower than in Europe.

Institutional Interpretations
...

  • Kuran's Perspective (Kuran 2011)
    • Islamic law institutions became growth hindrances.
    • Criticized stability of inheritance laws, lack of legal frameworks for firms, and inflexible religious trusts (waqfs).
  • Rubin's View (Rubin 2011)
    • Islam not inherently growth-restricting.
    • Interest constraints in Islam and Christianity compared.
    • Islamic bankers initially successful in circumventing interest constraints.
    • Rulers' need for religious legitimacy affected economic development.
    • Human capital development limited by economic segregation.
  • Role of Minorities (Pamuk and Williamson 2010)
    • Minorities (Greeks, Armenians, Jews, Christian Arabs) crucial in trade, finance, and industry.
    • European cooperation and protection benefited minorities.
    • Growing national aspirations marginalized minorities.
    • Majority's low regard for minorities delayed investment in trading skills.

Economic Development and Welfare in the Middle East (p 222-224)
...

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) Growth (Pamuk 2006) (p 223)
...

  • GDP per capita increased slowly from 1820 to 1913.
  • Middle Eastern GDP fell relative to US/western Europe.
  • Gulf region developed even slower, with Lebanon as the richest in 1913.

Standards of Living and Real Wages (Ozmucur and Pamuk 2002)
...

  • Real wages in large Middle Eastern cities were equal or higher than Europe.
  • Wealthier strata like merchants lagged behind Europe.

Welfare Analysis through Human Stature (Stegl and Baten 2009)
...

  • Anthropological measurements reflect health and nutrition.
  • People born 1850-1870 in Middle East had favorable nutrition compared to Europe.
  • Average heights decreased suddenly in 1880.
  • European stature exceeded Middle Eastern stature from then on.
  • Middle Eastern height lead vanished by the 20th century.

Regional Differences in Human Stature and Nutrition
...

Iraq (Stegl and Baten 2009) (P 224)
...

  • Desert vs. Other Inhabitants
    • Desert Bedouins were 0.85 cm taller on average.
    • Low population density in desert allowed better nutrition from stock breeding.

Turkey (Stegl and Baten 2009, Issawi 1980)
...

  • Western Coastal Areas vs. Central Anatolia
    • Shortest population in western coastal areas, now the richest regions.
    • Tallest people lived in central Anatolia due to low population density.
    • Central Anatolia specialized in cattle farming, providing protein-rich nutrition.

Egypt (Danubio et al. 2011) (P 225)
...

  • Coastal Region vs. Other Areas
    • Coastal inhabitants had greater height due to elevated rainfall.
    • Desert inhabitants taller than urban population.

Libya (Danubio et al. 2011)
...

  • Tuareg Nomads vs. Other Inhabitants
    • Nomadic Tuareg taller than other Libyans, especially oasis inhabitants.

Factors Influencing Height Trends (Stegl and Baten 2009)
...

  • Cattle Disease and Proximity to Animal Husbandry
    • Potential immediate cause of height drop in 1880s.
    • Middle Eastern populations benefited from proximity to animal husbandry.
  • Improvements in Nutrition and Public Health in Europe
    • Refrigeration technology facilitated food transport.
    • European urban populations became taller due to better nutrition and healthcare.
  • Historical Perspective
    • Mid-19th century Middle Eastern populations didn't suffer poor nutrition relative to Europeans.
    • Nomadic lifestyle provided economic advantages during this period.
  • Impact on Perception
    • Strong relative decline in 20th century stimulated perception of injustice among tribesmen.

The Middle East in the twentieth century (p 226)
...

European and Minority Dominance Before WWI (Issawi 1982)
...

  • Powerful positions held by Europeans, Armenians, Greeks, Christian minorities, and Jews.
  • Reversal of this development in the following century.
    • Europeans and minorities forced to leave or killed (e.g., Armenians in Turkey).
    • Abolition of privileges and nationalization of railways, banks, petrol stations.
  • Government ownership of mining and industries promoted during interwar and postwar years.
    • Nationalized industries struggled with quality and lacked new investments.

Impact on Specific Countries
...

  • Yemen

    • Loss of minority tradesmen hindered entrepreneurial development.
    • Low human capital status.

  • Turkey

    • Higher status of education and Ataturk's reforms promoted entrepreneurship.
    • Benefited from more equal gender distribution of education (Issawi 1982).

Ataturk's Reforms in Turkey (Ghanem 2014)
...

  • Political, economic, and cultural reforms.
    • Abolished sultanate and caliphate.
    • Transitioned to a secular state.
  • Positive influence on human capital.
    • Increased numeracy levels.
    • Mandatory and free primary schooling.
    • National education system replaced religious education.

Reindustrialization Efforts in the 20th Century
...

  • Political movements demanded political and industrial renaissance (Issawi 1982).
    • European imports disrupted during world wars.
    • Experimentation with new production methods.
  • Early industrial growth in Egypt and Turkey.
    • Turkey's active industrial policies and five-year plans in the 1930s.
  • State's role in industrial development due to lack of entrepreneurs.
    • Socialist ideas influential in Syria, Iraq, Algeria, Afghanistan, Egypt, Central Asia.

Industrial Development in Palestine and Israel
...

  • Sykes–Picot Agreement (1916)
    • Division of Ottoman Empire regions between England and France.
    • British mandate in Palestine (1920) and League of Nations decision for a Jewish national home (1922).
  • Jewish immigration increased entrepreneurial skills and industrial occupations.
    • Urban and industrial focus due to British restrictions on land purchases.
  • Resulted in economic growth despite political and humanitarian consequences.

Government-Run Firms in Other Middle Eastern Countries
...

  • Entrepreneurial elites were often foreigners or minorities.
    • Arab, Iranian, and Turkish politicians sought to reduce their influence.
  • Other reasons:
    • Lack of human capital and skills.
    • Peculiarities of oil production economies.

Skills and Human Capital Levels
...

  • Basic numeracy and literacy levels were low in the 19th century.
    • Strong improvement in early 20th century.
  • Low literacy rates (e.g., 7% in Egypt, 1907) and minimal public education funding.
    • Ottoman budget on education was 0.2% of total expenditure (1860/61).
    • Slightly higher in Algeria at 2% (1890–1914).
  • Low number of school years in early 20th century.
    • Higher in Israel (Barro and Lee 2013).
    • Yemen had almost no schooling.
    • Slightly better in Gulf States (Figure 7.7).

Impact of Oil Revenues
...

  • Oil discovery in 1908 transformed economies.
    • Initial small-scale use for camel sores.
    • 1870s: Primitive crude oil extraction in Iraq.
  • Significant development in the 1900s.
    • Iran granted a concession to a British company.
    • Concessions to European and American firms created an oligopoly.
  • Oil extraction primarily in Iran and Egypt pre-WWII.
    • 1940s: Iraq became a major exporter.
    • Bahrain and other countries increased exports.
  • Explosive growth post-1940.
    • Middle Eastern/North African oil production was below 5% of world production until 1940.
    • Rose to 26% in 1960 and 42% in 1975.
  • Direct payments to governments increased significantly (1950-1975).
    • From $240 million to $81 billion (to $163 billion in 1979).
  • Formation of OPEC in 1960.
    • Cartel policies during the Arab-Israel conflict of 1973 increased revenues.
    • Oil states benefited from a flood of revenues (Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, etc.).

Oil: Initial Benefits (1950s-1960s) (Issawi 1982)(P 230)
...

  • Stimulated economies, especially in least-developed countries.
    • Small part of the population worked in oil production (<2%).
    • In Gulf States, could be up to half the population.
  • Former oil company employees created their own firms.
    • On-the-job training fostered entrepreneurial skills and spirit.

Mixed Blessings from 1970s Oil Boom
...

  1. Resource Curse Phenomena

    • Difficult to agree on wealth distribution.
    • Wealth subjectively not 'earned' by population groups.

  2. Import Reliance

    • Belief that "anything could be imported."
    • Harmful to reindustrialization efforts.

  3. Unrealistic Income Expectations

    • Rising inequality led to dissatisfaction and potential civil conflict.
    • Example: Civil war in Algeria; extremist attitudes in other countries.

Government Strategy and Challenges
...
