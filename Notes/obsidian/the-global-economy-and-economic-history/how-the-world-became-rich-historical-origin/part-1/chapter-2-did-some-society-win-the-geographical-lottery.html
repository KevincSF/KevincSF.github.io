Geography and modern development
...

Geography matters for economic development today.

Coastline:
...

A country without coastlines would be unable to directly ship goods to other countries.

  • They also have to rely on expensive and hard-to-maintain road and rail networks which can be easily blocked or cut off by their neighbours.

Disease burden
...

Another important geographic factor is disease burden.

  • For instance, Countries in the "malaria belt" in sub-Saharan Africa continues to be underdeveloped
    • Malaria has probably killed the most people as a disease in human history
    • The burden of malaria is also economic. All else being equal, countries with a high proportion infected with malaria had growth rate that is 1.3% lower than other countries(ceteris paribus).
    • They also have higher infant mortality and lower investment in physical and human capital.

Evaluation of geographic explanation
...

Geography is not completely static, fisheries can be over-fished, societies need to adapt accordingly
Natural resources are generally not just there for taking, finding them requires exploration and the capacity to extract.

Gun, Germs and steel
...

With reference to Jared Diamond's book

Verticality of continent
...

Vertically aligned continents contains numerous micro-climates, which limits the spread of crops, domesticated animals and people.

  • According to diamond, the Near West was the birthplace of human civilization because:

    • It was where wheat, barley and pea were first cultivated
    • The rich rich fertile plains between tigris and Euphrates rivers were surrounded on several sides by so-called "hilly flacks"

Species:
...

  • In total, 33 of the world's 56 heaviest wild grasses originated in either Europe, the Neat East, or North Africa.
  • Only 1 for the domestic species(perhaps the most useless), the llama, originated in the new world.
  • 9 of the 14 large domesticable herbivores originated in Eurasia
  • Livestock are a tremendous source of animal power and protein
    • But domesticated animals are also a new source of disease.
    • The absence of domesticable animals make the new world vulnerable to disease

Climatic change
...

Climatic changes also played a role in the revival of the European economy after 1000. Cultivated land expanded, vineyards proliferated in England. Iceland and Greenland were settled by Vikings.
During a period of unusually warm weather, economic expansion, known as the Commercial Revolution, occurred between 1000 and 1300.

Geography and Transport infrastructure
...

Investment in transport infrastructure can overcome the curse of geography

  • The Trans-Siberian railway brought harsh terrain in contact with markets and goods.
  • Infrastructure projects can be financed by private individual or by the state
  • Why do some institutional arrangements facilitate private investment in infrastructure while other deter it? Why do some states invest and some doesn't?
    • These questions suggest that overcoming "bad" geography may require institutions capable of diverting resources to infrastructures
  • Although we would return to this issue later, but we will outline some ways investments in transport infrastructure affected historical economic development.
    • The Roman Empire invested in a road system. By the reign of the Emperor Trajan(97-117 CE). The road network comprised 80,000 km.
      • It was complemented by the massive inland waterways that crisscrossed the empire and connected landlocked cities with the Roman lake that was the Mediterranean Sea.
      • The Roman road system was built for military reasons.
        • But once stablished, it played a crucial role in knitting the empire together economically and culturally.
        • The suppression of Mediterranean piracy and the establishment of safe sea-lanes was also critical in lowering transport cost.
          • This enable interregional trade and specialization
        • Archaeological evidence attests to the fact that manufactured goods such as pottery were mass produced then transported
      • Following the conquest of the Mediterranean region, the Roman economy experienced economic growth by market expansion.
      • Adam Smith(1776/1976) observed that the greater the extent of the market, the greater the scope for specialization and division of labor
        • This suggests that an increase in market size can itself be a source of productivity improvement and economic growth.
    • Economic also matted for Chinese economic development. Economic growth in the Middle Kingdom was greatly aided by the creation of the Grand Canal by the Sui(581-618CE) and Tang(618-907CE) dynasty
      • It connected the Yangzi to Yellow River.
        • Its construction needed thousands of labor and the vase expenditures involved caused the collapse of the Sui
      • The canal transported grain to the capital city, Luoyang, and played a critical role in supplying the army guarding the northern frontier
      • In the Tang dynasty, 130,000 tons of grain were transported north each year.
      • Though the primary purpose of the Grand Canal were political and military, its creation brought economic benefits.
    • Transport infrastructure from industrializing Britain.
      • Before 1700, Britain was afflicted by extremely high transport costs.
      • By 1870, this situation has been transformed due to steam power and the introduction of railways.(Bogart, 2014;Bogart et al,2017; Bogart et al, 2017)
      • It was also due to investment in the road and canal network beginning in the 18th century.
  • The story of these investments in transport infrastructure is partly technological and partly institutional.
    • The institutional part of the story would be discussed in chapter 3

Geography and industrialisation
...

This chapter opened with the question: is geography fate?

  • We shown that it is not just fate, but there are undoubtedly matters for economic outcomes.
    Our final question we address is:
  • Can geography help explain why and where the Industrial Revolution took place?
  • Can the logic of Diamond's argument be used within Eurasia to explain why industrialisation first took off in Western Europe and not elsewhere?
    • With Eurasia, it has been suggested that EUrope's geographic position made it more likely to discover the Americas and benefit from Atlantic trade.

Within Europe, access to the Atlantic was a mixed blessing.
...

  • The discovery of the Americas and the development of new trade routes had varying effects on economic growth, depending on the society's political institution.
    • In other word, geography played a role in determining economic outcomes via its effect on institutions.
    • Long-distance commerce was already controlled by the crown, access to the Atlantic strengthen royal power.
  • While Spain was initially enriched by the Americas, the long-run impact on economic growth was negative, since it resulted in more extractive institutions.
  • In contrast, where monarchs lacked the ability to control or monopolize long-distance trade, the discovery of Americas strengthen the merchant class and allow them to constrained royal power

Energy
...

  • Perhaps the most direct impact of geography on economic growth is through the availability of natural resources.
    • 16th century England faced an energy crisis as the charcoal supplied by local forest could not meet the demand of growing urban population(Nef, 1932)
    • Coal relaxed this constrain, and cheap coal from north of England allow British economy to harness vastly more energy than previous after the improvement of steam engine.
    • Wrigley(1989,2010) argues that without coal there would have no industrial revolution.
      • Specifically, he distinguish between an
        • organic economy
          • In which energy is extracted from human or animal muscle or from wood
        • mineral economy
          • In which energy stored up over million of years became available for human use
