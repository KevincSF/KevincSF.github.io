Shadow case:

  • A important reason that some coutnries have taken a long time to catch up.

Flourishing Colonies with Enduring Institutions (p 199)
...

  • Labor-Scarce, Land-Abundant Colonies:
    • North America, Australia, New Zealand, and to a degree, Argentina flourished in the late 19th century.
    • Developed institutions following their parent countries, attracting migrants and capital.
  • Australian Institutional Development:
    • Despite harsh beginnings as a penal colony, Australia developed regional self-government.
    • Culminated in the establishment of the country as a constitutional monarchy in 1900.

Extractive Institutions and Lingering Effects
...

  • Persistent Extractive Institutions:
    • Many formerly colonized regions, especially in sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of Latin America, still lag behind.
    • Extractive institutions from colonial times persisted post-independence.
  • Nigeria's Case: (P 200)
    • Pre-colonial fragmentation and the legacy of the slave trade affected state formation negatively.
    • British colonial structures failed to centralize authority, develop an effective civil service, and enable direct population taxation. (Kohli, 2004, p. 292)
    • Post-independence, Nigeria faced civil conflicts and economic dependence on primary product exports, particularly oil.
  • Policy Choices Impact:
    • In Kenya, urban elites controlled marketing boards, extracting resources from farmers.
    • In Senegal, policies granted privileged access to subsidized inputs, enhancing political power but incurring economic costs (Bates, 1981, p. 111).

Ongoing Challenges and Limitations (p 200)
...

  • Post-Independence Challenges:
    • Urban elites' control in Kenya led to resource extraction from farmers.
    • In Senegal, privileged access policies enhanced political power but came with economic costs.
  • Violence and Governance Issues:
    • Ongoing violence and governance problems in Nigeria limit economic growth.
    • Sharp ethnic and religious divides hinder developmental programs.
  • Legacy of Policy Choices:
    • Economically costly policies for political expediency in Senegal and Kenya post-independence.

India's Historical Economic Trajectory
...

Economic Decline of British India
...

Historical Prosperity
...

  • Historical Prosperity:
    • India was a major textile producer, with per capita incomes at 60% of British levels around 1600 (Broadberry, Custodis, and Gupta, 2015, p. 67).
  • Decline in 17th-18th Centuries:
    • Urban population fell from 15% in 1600 to 8.7% by 1870.
    • Per capita GDP declined by about 30% during this period (Broadberry, Custodis, and Gupta, 2015, p. 67).
  • Mughal Empire's Prosperity Fading:
    • India relatively prosperous during Akbar's rule, but prosperity declined by the 18th century (Broadberry, Custodis, and Gupta, 2015, p. 71).

Factors Contributing to Economic Decline
...

  • Confluence of Factors:
    • Economic decline attributed to worsening climatic conditions, invasions, and endemic warfare (Clingingsmith and Williamson, 2008).
  • Nadir Shah's Sack (1739):
    • Delhi's sack by Nadir Shah resulted in significant casualties and economic devastation (Gupta, 2019, p. 808).
  • British East India Company:
    • British conquest involved prolonged warfare and pillaging, furthering economic decline (Gupta, 2019, p. 808).
    • Introduction of British cotton exports harmed India's textile industry, reliant on skilled labor (Gupta, 2019, p. 808).

British Raj Challenges
...

  • Missed Growth Opportunities:
    • British Raj, post-Indian Mutiny, saw increased total GDP but stagnant per capita incomes.
  • Capital Concentration on Railways:
    • British capital mainly invested in railways, neglecting other vital infrastructure (Gupta, 2019, p. 808).
  • Unintended Absentee Landlords:
    • Property rights attempts led to unintended creation of a class of absentee landlords (Gupta, 2019, p. 808).
  • Underdeveloped Factor Markets:
    • Factor markets, especially credit markets, remained undeveloped, and local farmers relied on moneylenders (Gupta, 2019, p. 808).

Path to Economic Stagnation
...

  • Stagnant Per Capita Incomes:
    • Despite British Raj, per capita incomes stagnated due to missed growth opportunities.
  • Neglected Infrastructure:
    • Railway-focused capital investment neglected other crucial infrastructure needs.
  • Unintended Social Consequences:
    • Property rights led to unintended consequences, creating challenges such as absentee landlords.
  • Underdeveloped Financial Markets:
    • Factor markets, especially credit markets, remained undeveloped, impacting local farmers (Gupta, 2019, p. 808).

British Raj Legacy (p 201)
...

  • Missed Growth Opportunities:
    • Establishment of British Raj after Indian Mutiny provided growth opportunities, but per capita incomes stagnated.
  • Railway-Centric Capital Investment:
    • British capital focused on railway construction, neglecting other infrastructure needs.
  • Neglected Infrastructure:
    • Other crucial infrastructure largely neglected, with improvements seen only in irrigation and fertilizers by the early 20th century.
  • Unintended Consequences of Property Rights:
    • Attempted property rights system created a class of absentee landlords (Gupta, 2019, p. 808).
  • Underdeveloped Factor Markets:
    • Factor markets, especially credit markets, remained undeveloped, leaving local farmers reliant on moneylenders.

Malthusian Economy and Demographic Challenges
...

  • Malthusian Economic Structure:
    • Trading regime imposed by the British left India as a producer of primary products and an importer of British manufactured goods (Galor and Mountford, 2008).
  • Population Growth Over Living Standards:
    • Increases in productivity translated into faster population growth rather than improving living standards.
  • Agricultural Investment Stall:
    • Investment in agriculture stalled, and education showed minimal improvement (Gupta, 2019, p. 805).
  • Low Literacy Rates at Independence:
    • At independence in 1947, only 17% of the population had basic literacy (Gupta, 2019, p. 805).

Post-Independence Economic Policies
...

  • Circumscribed Growth Opportunities:
    • Opportunities for economic growth circumscribed at independence.
  • Influence of Soviet Union's Model:
    • Indian economic policy influenced by the Soviet Union, leading to state-led industrialization and a focus on industrial self-sufficiency.
  • Technocratic Appeal of Planning:
    • Technocratic appeal of planning combined with a distrust of commerce rooted in the caste system.
  • Western Economic Advisors and Developmental Aid:
    • Policies backed by Western advisors like John Kenneth Galbraith and Joan Robinson (White, 2012, pp. 250–9).
    • Galbraith emphasized the need for planning in less developed countries (quoted in White, 2012, p. 253).

Disappointing Outcomes (1950–1980)
...

  • Limited Per Capita GDP Growth:
    • India's per capita GDP growth averaged 1.2% annually between 1950 and 1980 (World Bank, 2020a).
    • Lagged significantly behind leading East Asian economies.
  • Protectionist Policies and "License Raj":
    • Protectionist measures and the bureaucratic "License Raj" led to rent-seeking and corruption (White, 2012).
    • Import substitution and central planning yielded adverse consequences by the 1980s.

Shift to Liberalization (1990s Onward)
...

  • Deregulation Initiatives (1990s):
    • Indian government initiated economic deregulation in the 1990s.
    • Marked a crucial turning point in India's economic trajectory.
  • Encouraging Trends:
    • Liberalizing reforms facilitated economic growth.
    • India began to catch up with global economic trends.

Addressing Poverty in South Asia
...

  • Global Poverty Statistics (2015):
    • Worldwide, 731 million people lived in extreme poverty (<$1.90/day) in 2015 (World Bank, 2020a).
    • South Asia, with 216 million individuals, held a significant share.
  • Importance of Alleviating Poverty:
    • Alleviating poverty in South Asia is critical for global poverty eradication.
  • Positive Trends:
    • Trends are encouraging; liberalizing reforms since the 1990s have contributed to economic growth in India.

Comparison with China (Upcoming Discussion)
...

  • China's Role in Poverty Alleviation:
    • The upcoming discussion will explore China's strategies and contributions to poverty alleviation.
    • South Asia, including India, plays a crucial role in the broader context of global poverty reduction.

Japan's Meiji Restoration: Catalyst for Economic Growth
...

Meiji Restoration (1868): A Transformative Epoch
...

  • Objectives of Reforms:
    • Military Focus: Address Western imperialism by establishing a modern army and navy.
    • Comprehensive Reforms: Extended beyond military, reshaping Japanese society entirely.
  • Success in Conflicts:
    • Defeated China in the First Sino-Japanese War (1894–5).
    • Overcame Russia in the Russo-Japanese War (1904–5).

Meiji Era Challenges and Transformations
...

  • Pre-Meiji Society:
    • Class Structure: Tokugawa Japan (1603–1868) had rigid classes: samurai, farmers, artisans, merchants.
    • Fragmented Governance: No unified state; local lords (daimyo) ruled territories.
  • Restrictions on Trade:
    • Highly restricted trade with the outside world.
    • Dutch trading post at Dejima (Nagasaki) was the sole contact point with the West.
  • Economic Disparities:
    • Per capita GDP and real wages suggest relative poverty compared to 19th-century Europe or North America (Koyama, Moriguchi, and Sng, 2018).

Tokugawa Japan's Prewar Successes
...

  • Pre-Industrial Success:
    • Recent research indicates Tokugawa Japan's success as a pre-industrial economy.
    • Despite disparities, it demonstrated certain economic achievements.

Factors Enabling Meiji Success (p 202-203)
...

  • Diverse Reforms:
    • Military-Industrial Focus: Preparedness against Western powers.
    • Societal Changes: Altered traditional structures for modernization (Koyama, Moriguchi, and Sng, 2018). (P 203)
  • Meiji Economic Foundations:
    • Unified Governance: Transition from fragmented territories to centralized authority.
    • Trade Liberalization: Reforms facilitated broader international trade.
  • Global Recognition:
    • Japan's transformation gained recognition through victories in conflicts.

Lessons from Meiji Japan
...

  • Adaptive State-Building:
    • Swift adaptation to external challenges enabled Japan's success.
    • Comprehensive reforms impacted societal, economic, and military aspects.
  • Balancing Tradition and Modernity:
    • Meiji reforms demonstrated a balance between retaining aspects of tradition and embracing modernization (Koyama, Moriguchi, and Sng, 2018).

Ongoing Themes in Economic Development
...

  • Transformative Leadership:
    • Leadership during the Meiji era played a pivotal role in shaping Japan's economic trajectory.
    • Constant adaptation to global dynamics contributed to Japan's success.
  • Global Interconnectedness:
    • Embracing international trade and interactions was pivotal for Japan's economic growth.

Meiji Legacy
...

  • Modern Japan Emerges:
    • Meiji era laid the foundation for Japan's emergence as a modern, industrialized nation.
    • Legacy continues to influence Japan's global economic role (Koyama, Moriguchi, and Sng, 2018).

Unique Characteristics of Tokugawa Economic Development (p 204)
...

  • High Urbanization Rates (1800):
    • Urban population: 13%, towns with 10,000+ inhabitants.
    • Contrast with China (3–4%) (Vries, 2020).
  • Smithian Growth and Internal Trade:
    • Expansion of markets and internal trade drove Smithian growth.
    • Peasants increasingly participated in markets, intensifying agriculture (Sugihara, 2004).
  • Proto-Industrialization and Market Engagement:
    • Peasants involved in handicraft production for the market.
    • Parallel to proto-industrialization in early modern Europe.

Factor Markets and Property Rights
...

  • De Facto Property Rights for Peasants:
    • Though not formalized, peasants had de facto land rights.
    • Mortgage contracts facilitated land transfers for limited periods (Saito, 2009).
  • Distinct Factor Prices:
    • Japanese real wages lower than European standards.
    • Development path less favorable for labor-saving technologies like the spinning jenny.

Technology Adoption and Catch-up Growth
...

  • Incentives for Innovation:
    • Japanese context limited incentives for labor-intensive technological innovation.
    • Allen’s framework suggests less motivation for technologies economizing on land, energy, and capital.
  • Catch-up Growth Dynamics:
    • Rapid catch-up growth possible through technology adoption from leading countries ([Chapter 9]).

Institutional and Cultural Preconditions
...

  • Presence in Tokugawa Japan:
    • Factor markets and de facto property rights indicate certain preconditions.
    • Japanese context suited catch-up growth through technology transfer.
  • Adoption of Technology from Leading Countries
    • Institutional and cultural factors enabled Japan to adopt innovations from leading nations.

Evaluating Tokugawa Japan's Preparedness
...

  • Unique Developmental Path:
    • Distinct factor prices and proto-industrialization characterized Tokugawa's growth.
    • Notable readiness for technology adoption when needed.
  • Institutional and Cultural Adaptability:
    • Japan's ability to assimilate foreign technology showcased adaptability.
    • Cultural and institutional facets contributed to catch-up growth success.

Human Capital Development
...

  • High Levels in Pre-Industrial Japan:
    • Literacy and numeracy comparable to Western Europe.
    • Establishment of numerous commoner schools, domain schools, and private academies (Platt, 2004).
  • Facilitation of Second Industrial Revolution Technologies:
    • Abundance of educational institutions set the stage for technology adoption.

Factors Influencing Human Capital Levels
...

  • Effective Local Government:
    • Despite decentralization, local Japanese government effectively provided public goods.
    • Per capita tax revenues exceeded Qing China, fostering public goods provision (Sng and Moriguchi, 2014).
  • Local Lords' Investments:
    • Tokugawa Japan's local lords invested in public goods (e.g., road infrastructure, drainage).
    • Civil engineering projects expanded cultivation areas (Kanzaka, 2019).
  • Legacy of Institutional and Cultural Factors:
    • Tokugawa legacy marked by relatively high taxes and local government involvement.
    • Facilitated the subsequent successful state-building program.

Tokugawa Legacy and State-Building (p 205)
...

  • Institutional and Cultural Contributions:
    • High taxes and local government involvement formed a conducive environment.
    • Coalition succeeding Tokugawa Shogunate had favorable conditions for state-building (Koyama, Moriguchi, and Sng, 2018).
  • Political Revolution in 1868:
    • Implementation of state-building program required a national-level political revolution.

Evaluation and Synthesis
...

  • Human Capital and Economic Development:
    • Effective human capital investment in pre-Industrial Japan.
    • Legacy laid foundations for technological advancements.
  • Role of Institutions and Culture:
    • Effective local governance, high taxes, and public goods provision influenced human capital.
    • Tokugawa legacy contributed to a robust foundation for Japan's later state-building success.

Radical Transformation
...

  • Rapid state modernization post-Meiji Restoration.
  • Implemented compulsory education and significant tax hikes.
  • Violent suppression of revolts and uprising during tax protests.

Abolishing Samurai Class
...

  • Samurai class abolished, often without resistance.
  • Majority granted bonds instead of legal privileges.
  • Vries (2020) terms Meiji regime a "capitalist state."

Pro-Business Policies
...

  • Abolished guilds and local monopolies for a unified labor market.
  • Favored business in fiscal and regulatory policies.
  • Supported formation of large industrial conglomerates (zabatsu).

Non-Protectionist Approach
...

  • Couldn't impose high tariffs due to late Tokugawa treaties.
  • Integrated into the world economy, low average tariffs (<4%).
  • Free trade estimated to raise GDP by 8–9% (Bernhofen and Brown, 2005).

Export-Led Growth

Manufacturing Growth
...

  • Export-led growth predicated on expanding manufacturing.
  • Structure resembled industrial leaders by the early 20th century.
  • Manufacturing share in total output matched the US (Perkins and Tang, 2017).

Industrial Workforce Mobilization
...

  • Required high investment and mobilization of industrial workforce.
  • Rural workers subjected to factory discipline and long hours.
  • Large numbers of women entered the labor force, often facing harsh conditions.

Education's Role
...

  • Education played a crucial role in the industrialization process.
  • Prior high literacy levels; tertiary education lacking.
  • Meiji period introduced a three-tier educational system based on Western models.
  • Significant increase in students enrolled in higher scientific and technical education (Vries, 2020).

Japanese Educational Advantage and Industrialization (p 206)
...

  • Overcoming Wage Challenges
    • Japanese wages lower than Europe; hindered Western technology adoption.
    • Japanese creativity redesigned Western technology for cost-effectiveness (Allen, 2011a, p. 122).

Post-1867 Growth and Global Economy
...

  • Meiji Period Growth
    • Impressive, yet Japan struggled to fully catch up with Western economies.
    • Global economy based on free-flowing goods, capital, and people influenced Japan.
  • Economic Crisis and Imperial Pursuits
    • Japanese textile exports thrived pre-WWI but suffered during the Great Depression.
    • Crisis fueled imperial pursuits in Asia during World War II.

Post-War Growth and High-Tech Industries
...

  • Path to Recovery
    • Post-WWII recovery followed Meiji era, entering high-tech industries.
    • Skilled workforce granted Japan a comparative advantage (Ma, 2004).
  • 1980s Prosperity
    • By the 1980s, Japan became one of the world's leading economies.
      Note: Ma (2004) demonstrates similar developments in the silk industry. Allen (2011a, p. 122) provides insights on overcoming wage challenges.

How the East Asian Tigers Became Rich (p 207)

East Asian Tigers: Hong Kong, Singapore, South Korea and Taiwan.
...

Growed more compared to toher parts of the world.

  • Had relatively low income in the early 1960s.
  • Entered a period of civil war and military rule.
  • Sustained economic growth.

The East Asian Tigers' Unpredicted Prosperity and Catch-Up Growth (p 208)
...

  • Underestimated Success:
    • Forecasts by Paul Rosenstein-Rodan in 1961 predicted faster growth in South Asia and sub-Saharan Africa, underestimating Taiwan and South Korea.
    • Actual average growth rates for Taiwan and South Korea between 1961 and 1976 were 4.8% and 7.3%, respectively, significantly surpassing predictions (Acemoglu and Robinson, 2012a).
  • Unforeseen Success Factors:
    • Imported Ideas and Technologies:
      • Poor countries far from the technological frontier can achieve rapid economic growth by importing new ideas and technologies.
      • Catch-up potential should be even greater as technology advances in rich countries (Parente and Prescott, 2002).
    • Economic "Catch-Up":
      • Explains much of the rise of the East Asian Tigers.
      • Raises the question: Why were these countries successful in catching up while others remained poor?
  • Challenges in Understanding Catch-Up Growth:
    • Importing Technologies Not Enough:
      • Catch-up growth is not solely dependent on poor countries importing technologies from rich countries.
      • Example: Rapid spread of cellular phones and smartphones globally in the early 2000s.
    • Divergence Trend (1950-1990s):
      • Pritchett (1997) observed a dominant trend labeled as "divergence big time" between wealthy and poor countries.
  • Further Questions and Puzzles:
    • Exploring Prerequisites for Catch-Up Growth:
      • The puzzle remains: Why did some countries, like the East Asian Tigers, succeed in catching up while others did not?
      • A deeper understanding is required to unravel the factors that contribute to the prerequisites for catch-up growth.

Recent Economic Transformations in China (p 212)

Economic Progress and Poverty Alleviation
...

  • In 1990, a significant percentage of the Chinese population lived in extreme poverty.

Achievements and Ongoing Challenges

  • Over one billion people lifted from extreme poverty, marking a significant achievement.

Unraveling the Mystery of Delayed Industrialization
...

  • Historical focus shifts to the Qing dynasty (1644–1912) as a critical period.
  • Boom in the eighteenth century marked by population growth and a maturing market economy (Glahn, 2016).

Qing China: Favorable Economic Conditions and Institutional Landscape (p 213)
...

Pomeranz's Thesis and Subsequent Critiques
...

  • Pomeranz (2000) proposed no significant economic divergence between 18th-century Europe and China.
  • Later scholarship challenges this view, but Qing China's institutions were considered conducive to growth.

Adam Smith's Criteria for Economic Growth
...

  • Peace, easy taxes, and justice are prerequisites for growth (Stewart, 1793/1980).
  • Qing China in the 18th century seemingly fulfilled these conditions.

Factors Favoring Economic Growth in Qing China
...

  • Long periods of internal peace (1683-1796) promoted stability.
  • Large size and centralized governance facilitated market integration, evidenced by the Grand Canal's role (Glahn, 2016).

Low Taxation and Economic Regulation
...

  • Qing China experienced a decline in the tax burden, unlike Europe.
  • Merchants, organized through guilds, managed local commercial affairs.

Meritocratic Bureaucracy and Justice Administration
...

  • A meritocratic bureaucracy governed Qing China, seen as fair by contemporaries.
  • Chinese examination system influenced professional bureaucracies in Europe and the US.

Comparisons with European Governments
...

  • Chinese institutions were less intrusive and provided more public goods (Wong, 1997, 2012).
  • The examination system, initially praised for impartiality, influenced Western administrative practices.

Changing Dynamics: Corruption Over Time
...

  • Over time, evidence suggests increasing corruption in Chinese institutions (Sng, 2014).
  • Initial strengths gradually diminished, affecting the administration of justice.

China's Decline after 1750: Causes and Consequences (p 214)
...

Societal Turmoil and Foreign Aggression
...

  • Peasant rebellions, notably the Taiping Rebellion (1850–64), led to millions of deaths.
  • Defeats in the Opium Wars (1839–42, 1856–60) compelled China to cede Hong Kong and establish Western trading posts.

Proximate Causes and Deeper Roots
...

  • Political failures by Qing elites and a Malthusian crisis contributed to the collapse.
  • The underlying cause was the absence of sustained innovation, attributed to institutional and cultural factors.

Institutional Constraints on Innovation
...

  • China's political institutions hindered sustained innovation post-1750.
  • Emperor's lack of constraints contrasted with the autonomy of European cities and parliamentary contestation.

Europe's Fragmentation and Competitive Dynamics
...

  • Europe's fragmentation, despite trade barriers, facilitated competition and relocation of goods.
  • In contrast, imperial China lacked such freedoms, impeding innovation.

Meritocratic Bureaucracy and Disincentives
...

  • Despite meritocracy, imperial bureaucracy faced imperial discretion, discouraging excellence.
  • Wealthy and independent bureaucrats risked crackdowns, hindering innovation (Brandt, Ma, and Rawski, 2014; Ma and Rubin, 2019).

Centralized Governance and Hub-and-Spoke Structure
...

  • Centralized, hierarchical governance in China impeded information flow and innovation.
  • The hub-and-spoke structure, efficient for control, made the empire vulnerable to systemic collapse (Root, 2020).

Autocratic Shift and Literary Inquisitions
...

  • Qing rulers' autocratic governance, strengthening in the 17th and 18th centuries, limited innovation.
  • "Literary inquisitions" suppressed dissent and promoted a narrow understanding of China's intellectual history (Xue, 2021).

Constraints on Chinese Innovation: Qing Dynasty Dynamics (p 216)
...

Imperial Examination System and Cultural Constraints
...

  • Imperial examination system emphasized Confucian education, hindering practical applications (Huff, 1993; Lin, 1995).
  • Cultural norms favored existing knowledge; Confucianism dominated under the Qing dynasty.

The Self-Strengthening Movement and Confucian Context
...

  • Even attempts at reform (Self-Strengthening Movement, 1860–94) occurred within a Confucian framework.
  • Emphasis on classical education over practical skills; limited impact on innovation (Wright, 1957).

Lack of Innovation Catalysts: Missing Coordinating Mechanism
...

  • Absence of competition and innovation forum hindered scientific and technological advancements.
  • China lacked a unifying coordinating mechanism, unlike the competitive markets in Europe (Mokyr, 2016).

Clan-Based Culture and Institutional Development
...

  • Clan-based nature of Chinese families provided a safety net but limited institutional development.
  • In medieval Western Europe, institutions like guilds and communes emerged due to the absence of the clan.

Impact on Banking and Finance
...

  • Clan-based support reduced the need for interpersonal risk-sharing in China.
  • Banking and finance emerged in the West due to the absence of the clan, fostering risk-sharing institutions (Chen, Ma, and Sinclair, forthcoming).

Demographic Factors and Malthusian Perspective
...

  • High birth rates, multi-generational families, and early marriage characterized China's demography.
  • Malthusian perspective: High population growth outpaced advancements, contributing to persistent poverty (Brandt, Ma, and Rawski, 2014).

One-Child Policy and Historical Demographic Patterns
...

  • Introduction of the one-child policy in 1979 marked a shift in China's demographic strategy.
  • Historical patterns included high birth rates, high death rates, and limited per capita income growth until the 20th century.

Exploring China's Rapid Growth: Autocracy and Historical Context (p 216)
...

Remarkable Transformation and the Question Posed
...

  • China's rapid growth lifted nearly one billion people out of extreme poverty.
  • The focus shifts to understanding the factors behind China's remarkable rise.

Autocratic Continuity Despite Historical Challenges
...

  • Despite challenges post-Opium Wars and Qing fall, China remained an autocracy.
  • Republican period (1912–49) saw initial state-building and industrialization efforts.

Communist Period Achievements and Human Costs
...

  • Communist period (1949–) built a modern centralized state, with achievements.
  • Forced collectivization, Five-Year Plans, and the Great Leap Forward resulted in a devastating famine.

Shift from Market Economy: Consequences
...

  • Shift away from a market economy impacted industrialization dynamics.
  • In a market economy, industrialization was constrained by agricultural productivity, avoiding imbalance.

The Impact of Communist Policies
...

  • Forced collectivization disrupted traditional market dynamics.
  • Excessive urbanization strained food supply, leading to rising prices and slowed industrialization.

The Challenge of Balancing Industrialization and Agriculture
...

  • In a market economy, industrialization pace balanced by agricultural productivity.
  • Excessive urbanization's impact on food supply and prices demonstrated the challenge under autocratic policies.

Commuism (P 217)
...

Tragedy of the Great Leap Forward: Autocratic Policies and Consequences
...

Misguided Attempt to Accelerate Industrialization
...

  • Mao and advisors bypassed the market, aimed to increase agricultural productivity through collectivization.
  • Perceived inefficiencies and hoarding led to disastrous policies during the Great Leap Forward.

Lack of Checks and Balances: Magnifying the Tragedy (P 218)
...

  • Broader political system lacked checks and balances, crucial in amplifying the scale of the disaster.
  • Overoptimistic grain production estimates, inflexible procurement, and unwillingness to recognize issues led to catastrophe.

Role of Local Bureaucrats and Violence
...

  • Local bureaucrats had incentives to inflate estimates, contributing to overoptimistic projections.
  • Extreme violence used to enforce quotas; accusations of hoarding or shirking resulted in beatings and killings.

Productivity Loss and Human Cost
...

  • Collectivization led to productivity loss; peasants' local knowledge diminished.
  • Severe famine occurred in areas of previously high agricultural productivity (Meng, Qian, and Yared, 2015).

Human Toll and Covered-Up Starvation
...

  • Debate over death toll during the Great Famine; estimates range from 15 to 45 million.
  • Political elites covered up news of mass starvation, resulting in one of the most destructive episodes in human history.

Political Institutions and Autocracy
...

  • Autocratic and centralized state magnified economically destructive policies.
  • Autocracy contributed to the disasters of the Great Leap Forward, Great Famine, and Cultural Revolution.

Reversal Under Deng Xiaoping
...

  • Despite earlier disasters, Deng Xiaoping initiated market-oriented reforms in 1979.
  • Autocratic rule, while contributing to past tragedies, also enabled a successful reversal and subsequent economic growth.

China's Unique Path to Modernization: Economic Reforms and Growth (p 217)
...

Executive Power Constraints: A Contrast with East Asian Tigers
...

  • East Asian Tigers benefited from small size, allowing private enterprise to constrain autocratic tendencies.
  • China, with a powerful central government, lacked formal constraints on executive power.

Borrowing Industrial Inputs and Managerial Know-How
...

  • China, like Japan and the East Asian Tigers, didn't need to reinvent industrialization.
  • Opened up to foreign direct investment, borrowing inputs and managerial know-how.

Economic Potential and Surplus Labor
...

  • Pre-reform China had tremendous economic potential with over one billion people in subsistence farming.
  • Large labor force wasn't the sole factor; surplus labor existed before the 1980s in developing economies.

Reform Periods: 1978-1995 and Post-1995
...

  • Early reforms (1978-1995) dismantled aspects of the command economy.
  • Post-1995 reforms marked another phase in China's economic transformation (Brandt, Ma, and Rawski, 2017).

Reversing Damaging Policies: Agriculture
...

  • First reforms involved dismantling damaging collectivization and rural industrialization.
  • Revival of household farming and agricultural productivity increases freed millions for industrial labor.

Economic Liberalization's Impact (P 218)
...

  • Grain output rose significantly (almost one-third) between 1978 and 1984 (Brandt, Ma, and Rawski, 2014).
  • Increased agricultural productivity allowed for the migration of millions into industrial sectors.

China's Unique Growth Trajectory
...

  • China's economic reforms, divided into two periods, played a pivotal role in its unique growth trajectory.
  • The transformation involved overcoming damaging policies, embracing liberalization, and utilizing surplus labor.

Post-1995 Reforms and Legal Development (p 218)
...

Widespread Privatization and Legal Protections
...

  • Post-1995 reforms included significant privatization, reducing the state's share of industrial output.
  • Legal protection for private enterprise expanded, including the right to sue the government.

Legal System Development and German Civil Law Influence
...

  • Pre-reform China lacked the rule of law; the introduction of a market economy necessitated legal development.
  • Adoption of German civil law elements, allowing private citizens to sue the government (Fukuyama, 2014).

Unique Property Rights System
...

  • Despite Communist opposition to private property, China created a system of usufructuary rights.
  • These rights, technically leases with up to seventy years, could be bought, sold, mortgaged, or transferred (Fukuyama, 2014).

Gradual Move Towards Rule of Law
...

  • China didn't adopt Western institutions entirely; minor moves towards rule of law induced radical economic and societal transformation.

Increased Openness to Global Trade (P 219)
...

Economic Zones and Foreign Direct Investment
...

  • China's increased openness to global trade involved establishing economic zones.
  • Foreign direct investment surged, contributing to catch-up growth opportunities.

Trade Expansion and Growth Acceleration
...

  • China's trade ratio surged from 9.7% to 31.9% between 1978 and 1993 (Brandt, Ma, and Rawski, 2014).
  • Growth accelerated in the 1990s and 2000s due to reforms restructuring the public sector.

Catch-Up Growth and Economic Restructuring
...

  • China's catch-up growth model involved bridging the gap with the world's economic frontier.
  • Foreign investment and efficiency improvements in state enterprises contributed to growth.

Predictable Slowdown in Growth
...

  • Chinese growth slowed post-2000s, aligning with catch-up growth predictions.
  • From around 10% per year, growth now stabilizes at 6–7%, reflecting the maturation of the catch-up growth model.

Factors Influencing China's Modern Growth (p 219)
...

Institutional Legacy
...

  • Chinese historical legitimacy tied to "Mandate of Heaven" influenced Communist rule (Weber, 2021).
  • Deng Xiaoping's adoption of "good rule means legitimate rule" addressed legitimacy deficit.
  • Autocratic governance's potential impact on economic growth and party legitimacy.

Contemporary Challenges
...

  • Xi Jinping's presidency since 2013: Strengthening autocracy, moving away from the market.
  • Persistent risk of economic policy reversal due to centralized and autocratic governance.

Cultural Contribution
...

  • Pre-modern China's emphasis on education for economic and social advancement.
  • Legacy of high educational attainment contributing to sustained economic success (Chen, Kung, and Ma, 2020).

Lessons from Chinese History
...

  • China's unique path to wealth without 18th-century England or 19th-century US attributes.
  • Institutions' critical role in economic growth, challenging the Washington Consensus (Weber, 2021).
  • Institutional reforms context-dependent and politically constrained.

Key Takeaway
...

  • Learning from history's potential and limits in understanding global economic transformations.
  • Success in wealth creation linked to cultural, historical, and institutional contexts.
  • Relevance of past lessons, but a need for flexibility in adopting institutional reforms.
