Capitalist Agriculture

Characteristics of English Agriculture Preceding the Industrial Revolution
...

  • Highly commercialized relative to the rest of Europe.
  • Geared towards the market with a majority of workers as wage laborers.
  • Farms were large compared to continental Europe, allowing for economies of scale.
  • England became a major exporter of food by the mid-18th century.
  • Many historians view this distinctive "capitalist" agriculture as a precondition for the Industrial Revolution.

Labor Productivity in English Agriculture (pg 153)
...

  • Comparative high labor productivity before the Industrial Revolution.
  • Output per worker similar to Italy and France in 1500.
  • By 1750, higher in England and in the Low Countries than elsewhere in Europe.

Factors Contributing to Increased Productivity
...

  • Medieval England's Open-Field System (McCloskey, 1976)
    • Dominant form of farming was small-scale agriculture organized around open fields and commons.
    • Peasants farmed small, scattered plots across the manor.
    • Enabled risk-sharing among farmers to mitigate idiosyncratic risks like hailstorms, floods, or crop blight (McCloskey, 1976).
    • Limited harm from local shocks.

Downsides of the Open-Field System
...

  • Challenges and Limitations
    • Costly and time-consuming to move between different plots of land.
    • Small, disjoint, and irregular plots made it unprofitable to introduce new crop types or equipment.

Significance
...

  • Understanding the distinctive characteristics of English agriculture, its shift towards commercialization, and the role of the open-field system provides insights into the economic conditions that laid the groundwork for the Industrial Revolution.

Institutional Constraints and Industrialization
...

Impact of British Political Institutions
...
  • By the mid-18th century, British citizens felt relatively secure about their property, innovations, and industrial output.
    • Assurance that their assets would not be seized by the government or any coercive power.
    • British political institutions, although imperfect and corrupt by modern standards, provided a level of constraint on executive power.
    • Majority of the population reasonably protected from infringements on property rights (Balla and Johnson, 2009; Kuran, 2011; Ma and Rubin, 2019).
Comparison with Other Parts of the World
...

  • Autocratic Governance

    • In contrast to Britain, parts of the world with autocratic governance faced challenges.

  • European Context

    • Even within Europe, Spain exhibited a willingness to transgress property rights.
      • 16th-century kings Charles V and Philip II regularly reneged on debts and confiscated treasure from the Atlantic trade.
      • Privileges were given to foreign merchants over domestic ones.
      • Rights of religious minorities were trampled upon (Drelichman, 2005; Drelichman and Voth, 2011).
Encouraging Domestic Production
...
  • The infringements on property rights in other parts of the world, especially autocratic regimes and certain European powers, did not encourage domestic production on the scale seen in Britain.

Does the Transatlantic slave trade explains Britain's industrialization? (pg 158)
...

European Colonization and the British Industrial Revolution
...

Williams (1944)
...

  • Popularized the link between the slave trade and commercial and financial development in 18th-century Britain (Williams, 1944).
  • Argued that profits from the slave trade played a crucial role in fueling the Industrial Revolution.
    • Emphasized the trade nexus connecting British manufactured exports, the West African slave trade, and plantations in the Americas.

Inikori (2002)
...

  • Presented a powerful argument connecting European colonization with the Industrial Revolution (Inikori, 2002).

Trade Nexus Described by Solow (1987)
...

  • Described the period before the Industrial Revolution:
    • Total trade increased significantly.
    • British ships crisscrossed the Atlantic, trading manufactured goods with various regions.
    • Atlantic islands exported wine, Africa slaves, Brazil gold, and the West Indies sugar and molasses.
    • Some British North American colonies traded rice and tobacco to Britain and bought British manufactures.
    • All these flows depended on the product of slave labor (Solow, 1987, p. 732).

Role of Slavery
...

  • Slavery was central to the trade nexus.
  • Sugar plantations, dependent on slave labor, had harsh conditions, making it challenging to attract free workers.
  • West Indies transformed into slave economies by the 18th century.
  • Slavery allowed plantation owners to extract the entire surplus generated by forced labor in a harsh climate (Wright, 2020).

Neglected Facts in Traditional Accounts
...

  • Traditional accounts of the 18th-century British economy often neglected the centrality of slavery.
  • Williams's argument about slave trade profits fueling industrialization established a crucial correlation, prompting the question of causation.

Causal Relationship Question
...

  • Williams's correlation between slave trade profits and industrialization raises the question of causation.

Challenges in Establishing a Direct Link (pg 159)
...

  • Scholars following Williams have not established a direct link from investors and landowners benefiting from slave labor in the Caribbean to the innovative developments in the north of England.

Reasons for the Lack of Direct Link
...

  1. Integration with Capital Markets
    • Entrepreneurs, factory owners, and inventors of the Industrial Revolution funded their firms from profits and personal savings.
    • Not well integrated with London-based capital markets.
    • Sugar, while crucial to specific industries, was less connected to the overall economy than coal, iron, and textiles (Eltis and Engerman, 2000, p. 140).
  2. Competition and Industry Size
    • Super-normal profits were not sustained in the long run due to competition.
    • Sugar industry was small relative to the British economy as a whole (Eltis and Engerman, 2000).
    • Direct contribution of slavery to the British economy was small in simple accounting terms.

Strategic Importance of the Slave Trade
...

  • Scholars, including Solow (1987), suggest broader connections between slavery and the wider British economy.
  • Slave trade ensured an elastic supply of strategic goods like sugar and tea, contributing to an "industrious revolution."
  • Derenoncourt (2019) finds that cities linked with the transatlantic slave trade grew faster than others between 1600 and 1850.
  • Slavery raised the return on investment across the empire, possibly enabling faster structural change and industrial growth.

Inikori's Focus on Overseas Trade (pg 160)
...

  • Inikori (2002) emphasizes the role of sugar and slavery in the rise of overseas trade in the late 17th and early 18th centuries.
  • Expansion in overseas trade drove initial increases in productivity, and technological progress built on this foundation.

Assessing Arguments and Quantitative Economic Significance
...

  • Modern assessments align with Solow's view that slavery wasn't the cause but might have been important for British economic growth (Solow, 1987, p. 733).
  • Theoretical frameworks proposed by Inikori (2002) and Darity (1992) stress linkages between overseas trade and domestic manufacturing.
  • However, evidence for these connections remains inconclusive, requiring further quantitative assessment.

Background Role of Atlantic Slavery
...

  • Eltis and Engerman (2000, p. 138) emphasize that no industry was indispensable to the Industrial Revolution.
  • Atlantic slavery, while not directly causing the Industrial Revolution, was a background condition tied to Britain’s rise to economic and military prominence in the 18th century.

Historical Perspectives

Transport Improvements and Free Internal Trade

Market Integration Not Unique to Britain

Apprenticeship System in Britain (pg 164)
...

  • Private Contracts: Apprenticeship contracts in Britain were private and voluntarily enforced but regulated by municipal authorities.
  • Overcoming Human Capital Market Problem(Humphries, 2010): Addressed the problem of how talented but poor individuals could acquire training by locking apprentices into contracts where they provided labor in return for bed, board, education, and training .
  • Recovery of Training Costs: As apprentices became more skilled, their labor became more valuable, allowing masters to recoup the costs of training.
  • Comparison with Europe (Wallis, 2008): Apprenticeship was not unique to Britain but differed as it was not tied to guilds, thriving despite them.
  • Fluid Labor Market(Minns and Wallis, 2012; de la Croix, Doepke, and Mokyr, 2017): Apprentices often left contracts early, and journeymen transferred skills from town to town in a fluid labor market .
  • Widespread Skill Diffusion(van Zanden, 2009): Movement of labor and a fluid market in London facilitated widespread diffusion of craft skills, explaining Britain’s high human capital and relatively low wage differences between skilled and unskilled workers .

  • Political Institutions:
    • Protected property rights with the flexibility for adjustment and renegotiation.
    • Open to innovation and invention.
    • Encouraged domestic trade despite mercantilist policies.
  • Access to Atlantic and Slave Economies:
    • Benefited from access to the Atlantic and slave economies.
    • Avoided the resource curse associated with such access.
  • Labor Market Institutions:
    • Avoided costs linked to guild privileges.
    • Provided skills and training on a large scale.
  • Comparison with Other Regions:
    • Holy Roman Empire: Free cities had skilled laborers.
    • Dutch Republic: Had secure property rights and access to international markets.
    • Spanish and Portuguese: Possessed worldwide colonial empires.
  • Comprehensive Factors:
    • The uniqueness lay in Britain having all these factors.
    • Removal of any one factor might have hindered industrialization.

Mokyr(2009,2016)
...

  • Offered a theory addressing precisely this puzzle.
    • Emphasizes the role of cultural elites.
  • Mokyr(2016)
    • Emphasizes the role played by the Republic of Letters in producing a culture that rewarded and encouraged innovation.
    • The republic of letters was a Europe-wide forum in which Enlightenment elites disseminated and disputed the cutting -edge scientify findings and philosophical insights of the day.(p 172)
      • Provide a setting for ideas to be tested
      • Norms of good behaviors and scientific standars to develop.
    • In previous eras and in other societies, innovators are often siloed.
      • Often work in isolation or dependent on the patronage of single individual.
      • Individual genius was not enough to produce a culture of innovation, and patronage systems of pre-modern wwas not conductive for innovative new ideas.
  • Great individuals and scientists is not enough on their own(pg 173)
    • Culture of growth is needed which allow secondary figures to play crucial role in disseminating the new ideology of progress.
  • The culture of growth cannot be assessed in isolatiion.
    • Mokyr(2016) emphasize that the emergence of a Europe-wode postal service as key to scholoarly communication.
  • Political fragmentataion was also important.
    • Cultural unity with political fragmentation.
      • Fragmentation coinicided with thriviing intellectual culture that spanned political borders.
      • The political division of Europe meant that innovative and heretical thinkers had an avenue of escape from heavy-handed political authorities.

  • Political Fragmentation and Cultural Unity in Europe

  • Europe's uniqueness during the Industrial Revolution resulted from the combination of cultural unity with political fragmentation (Mokyr, 2009).
  • Political divisions allowed innovative thinkers to escape heavy-handed authorities, preserving ideas and innovations.
  • Europe's cultural unity enabled inventors to build upon the advances of the Scientific Revolution.
  • The interconnectivity fostered by the Republic of Letters contributed to a continent-wide Enlightenment Culture.

Preconditions for Industrialization and the Role of the Republic of Letters
...

  • Skilled craftsmen, artisans, and tinkerers were essential for putting Enlightenment ideas into practice (Mokyr, 2009).
  • England had an abundance of skilled workers, while other parts of the world lacked a comparable number of highly skilled mechanical workers.
  • Political fragmentation in the Middle East and India did not promote innovation, while political unity in Qing China stifled free thinking.

British Skilled Workers and the Industrial Enlightenment
...

  • British skilled workers internalized key Enlightenment ideas, including the belief in transforming the world for the betterment of humankind.
  • This mentality played a crucial role in driving technological change during the Industrial Revolution.
  • Innovators of the period contributed to various fields beyond their specialization, exemplifying a mindset of improvement.
  • The culmination of these ideals gave rise to an "Industrial Enlightenment" centered in Britain (Mokyr, 2009).

Reconciling Views on the Origins of the Industrial Revolution
...

  • Technological change during the era could have been both labor-saving and a response to high labor costs (Crafts, 2011).
  • A cultural transformation, as proposed by Mokyr, played a critical role in increasing the supply of inventors, tinkerers, and entrepreneurs.
  • Both arguments shed light on why Britain became the center of industrialization.
