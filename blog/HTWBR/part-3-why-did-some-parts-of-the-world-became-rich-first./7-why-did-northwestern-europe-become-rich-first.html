How Geography shaped instituional development
...

Resource curse: geographic endowments can be a detriment to economic development(pg 132)
...

  • It is not always obvious that “good” geographic endowments lead to economic success.
    • Good geographic endowments can sometimes be a detriment to economic development.
    • Large literature on the "resource curse" (Sachs and Warner, 2001; Mehlum, Moene, and Torvik, 2006; Robinson, Torvik, and Verdier, 2006; Ross, 2015; Desierto, 2018).
    • Resource curse explains why countries rich in resources can still be very poor.
    • Particularly problematic in sub-Saharan Africa.
    • The answer lies primarily in a society’s political institutions.
      • Where political institutions are extractive, natural resources strengthen ruling elites’ grip on power.
      • They use resources to pay off friends and enrich themselves, leading to more extractive institutions.
    • In short, natural resources can be a blessing or a curse, depending on political institutions.

The resource curse is an example of how geography and institutions interact.
...

  • Geography can lead to institutional developments that promote or hinder economic growth.
  • One example is the “fractured land” hypothesis (Diamond, 1997; Jones, 2003).
    • Europe's persistent political fragmentation is explained by its geography.
    • Europe's mountain ranges and natural barriers contributed to distinct geographic cores, influencing future states.
    • Alps separate the Italian Peninsula, Pyrenees separate the Iberian Peninsula.
    • China, dominated by a single large northern plain between the Yangtze and the Yellow Rivers.
    • Presence of a core region of high land productivity in China crucial for political unification.
    • Lack thereof in Europe led to fragmentation (Fernández-Villaverde et al., 2020).

Another factor explaining Europe’s fragmentation is the location of the Eurasian steppe.
...

  • Eurasian steppe: vast grassland linking Eastern Europe and East Asia.
    • Steppe nomads, after domesticating horses, posed a perennial threat to sedentary populations.
    • Fragile ecology of the steppe made nomads likely to invade during periods of drought or cold weather.
  • China’s proximity to the steppe necessitated the rise of a strong, northern-based state.
    • Such a state would protect against steppe threats and be powerful enough to unify the rest of China (Ko, Koyama, and Sng, 2018).

Why Was There No Medieval European Take-off?(pg 134)
...

Emergence of Self-Governing Cities in the Carolingian Empire (pg 134)
...

  • During the 11th and 12th centuries, self-governing or independent cities emerged on the edges of what had been the Carolingian Empire.

Characteristics of the New Cities (pg 134)
...

  • The new cities served as manufacturing centers and trade entrepôts.
    • Trading networks based on the market economy developed without a central authority.
    • McCloskey (2006) identifies these cities as having "bourgeois values."

Political Polycentricity in Europe (pg 134)
...

  • Unlike other regions, Europe's political polycentricity allowed for multiple sources of power.
    • The Catholic Church assumed an unprecedented political role.
    • Fukuyama (2011)
      • highlights the Church's role in undermining claims of clans and extended families.
    • Siedentop (2014)
      • sees Christianity as critical to the emergence of liberal societies.
    • Research by Schulz, Bahramni-Rad, Beauchamp, and Henrich (2019); Henrich (2020); and Schulz (2020) supports the claim that the Church undermined traditional kinship networks
    • The Church's prohibition on cousin marriage, according to the research, contributed to the formation of medieval communes.

Birth of Medieval City-States (pg 134)
...

  • Medieval communes were the birthplaces of great medieval city-states.
    • These city-states played a crucial role in relieving Europe of its post-Roman economic challenges.
    • The emergence of independent, free cities was unlikely under the rule of a strong centralized ruler typical in other parts of Eurasia.

Challenges to Sustained Economic Growth (pg 134)
...

  • Despite several centuries of growth during the Commercial Revolution, medieval Europe faced challenges to sustained economic growth.
    • Questions arise about the factors inhibiting the full potential of the medieval European economy.
    • Consideration is given to why the preconditions for sustained economic growth did not lead to a continuous takeoff during the medieval period.

Challenges in Historical Analysis (pg 135)
...

Limitations of Certainty
...
  • Answering historical questions with certainty is challenging due to the lack of controlled experiments in history.
    • Example: Uncertainty about how the European economy would have developed without the Black Death.
Impact of the Black Death
...
  • The Black Death had devastating immediate effects on trade and the medieval economy.
  • Positive institutional implications may have emerged in the long run, but the immediate impact was severe.
  • Possibility of a pre-existing crisis in the medieval economy before the Black Death (Postan, 1973).

Medieval Economic Growth (pg 135)
...

Occurrence and Characteristics
...
  • Economic growth did occur in the Middle Ages, but it was not sustained and prone to reversals.
  • Trade and market expansion were crucial for economic growth during the Commercial Revolution (Van Zanden & Prak, 2006).
  • Medieval city-states, such as Florence, Genoa, and Venice, experienced rapid growth in the 12th and 13th centuries.
Factors Influencing Growth
...
  • Creation of political communities based on trust and cooperation in city-states (Van Zanden & Prak, 2006).
  • Overcoming free-rider and agency problems through active citizenship.
  • Enforcing property rights and providing market-supporting public goods.

Challenges to Sustained Growth (pg 135)
...

Evidence of Economic Decline
...
  • Even before the Black Death, evidence suggests a decline in the economic dynamism of city-states.
  • Growth in city-states subject to reversals, and autonomous city-states grew more slowly over time (Stasavage, 2014).
Barriers to Sustained Growth
...
  • Merchant oligarchs in city-states imposed barriers to entry, hindering sustained growth.
  • Transition to a fully open-access social order did not occur (Stasavage, 2014).
  • Rent-seeking ruling elite contributed to the decline of medieval city-states.

Case Study: Florence's Decline (pg 135)
...

Illustrative Example
...
  • Florence experienced economic decline despite remaining a center for international trade and banking in the 15th century.
  • Peak as a manufacturing center occurred a century earlier (Epstein, 1991, 2000).
Rent-Seeking and Oligarchy
...
  • Manipulation and restriction of market access by Florence to maintain regional primacy.
  • Imposition of large and distortionary taxes on the peasantry.
  • Financial services monopolized by a few banking families, including the powerful Medici (Belloc et al., 2021).
Political Institutions and Decline
...
  • Competitive political institutions in Florence were subverted by the Medici in the 15th century (Belloc et al., 2021).
  • Consequences of a rent-seeking ruling elite undermining the city-state's own prosperity.

  • The labour scarcity result that catastrophe encourged women to enter the workforce, producing an active market for agriculutral labour.

  • Demographic factors alone were not sufficient for sustained economic growth.

  • The timing of modern economic growth and the Industrial Revolution is not easily explained solely by demographics.

  • If demographic factors were the sole driver, northern Germany's economic stagnation would contradict expectations based on favorable demographics.
  • Economic development is a complex interplay of various factors beyond demographics.

Parliament and the rise of limited, representative government

Dutch Economic success: Parliament:(pg 141)
...

Created instituions that benefited members of the economic elite.

  • A period of Dutch economic development in the century following 1580
  • Real wage grew faster than the rest of Europe
  • Increased urbanization, large-scale investments in canal and other inland transport
  • Amsterdam became the European financial center

Success and instituional change: (pg 142)
...

  • The success was a direct result of intitutional change.
  • The rise of limited, representatiev governance was one of the key driving forces behind the Dutch economic expansion.
  • No centralized authorities to extract resources, trample over property right, or infringe personal liberties.
  • Although it might favor some interest over others, each of the seven provinces retained considerable powwer.
  • Limited governmant:
    • An important precondition for long-run economic growth
      • Not enough on its own.
    • The Dutch ended up being relatively late to the industrailization game

England: Intitutional change prior to industrial revolution.
...

Labor market change: (pg 143)(Ogilvie, 2019)
...

  • Played a important role in liberating the domestic economy.
  • The medieval economy was dominated by craft and merchant guilds that regulated entry.
    • By 1700, the power of English guilds had greatly diminished.
    • Britain was quite different from much of continental Europe
    • where guild power remained entrenched.
      • This hampered economic development by allowing insiders to block competition and innovation .

Emergence of Parliament in Medieval England:
...

Background:
...
  • The centuries leading up to modern economic growth saw the emergence of Parliament as the dominant legal and political force in England (Greif and Rubin, 2021).
Representation of Urban Interests:
...
  • Parliament, solidified in the reign of Edward I (r. 1272–1307), began to represent urban interests and not just the nobility.
Parliament Frequency in Middle Ages:
...
  • As shown in Figure 3.8, the English Parliament met frequently during the Middle Ages, reflecting the frequency of wars and the crown's need for additional revenues.
Dominance of Strong Medieval Kings:
...
  • Strong medieval kings typically had the ability to dominate Parliament, and challenges to royal authority usually came from the nobility with private armies rather than Parliament.

Transformation of Parliamentary Power
...

Late 15th Century Changes:
...
  • The institutional arrangement began to change in the late 15th century, particularly after the Wars of the Roses (1455–85).
  • Henry VII (r. 1485–1509) turned to Parliament to bolster his legitimacy, ruling by acts of Parliament more than his predecessors (Greif and Rubin, 2021).
Weakening Noble Families:
...
  • Henry VII weakened the power of noble families, reducing potential challenges to his rule (Penn, 2011).
Henry VIII's Increased Reliance
...
  • Henry VIII (r. 1509–47) relied on Parliament even more frequently, especially after the Reformation weakened the legitimacy of the Catholic Church.
Successors' Dependency on Parliament:
...
  • Henry VIII's successors, Edward VI (r. 1547–53), Mary I (r. 1553–8), and Elizabeth I (r. 1558–1603), heavily relied on Parliament due to low legitimacy.

Rise of Parliament's Power
...

Political Institutions Divergence (17th Century):
...
  • As recent research confirms, it was in the 17th century that England’s political institutions diverged from those of continental Europe (Henriques and Palma, 2020).
English Civil Wars (1642–51):
...
  • The English Civil Wars (1642–51) pitted Parliamentarians against supporters of King Charles I (r. 1625–49).
  • Conflict primarily centered on the proper institutional structure of English governance (Greif and Rubin, 2021).
Key Question:
...
  • Did a king have the legitimate right to rule without Parliament or was following the law, as created by Acts of Parliament, essential to being a legitimate king?
Tudors' Move Towards Parliamentary Legitimacy:
...
  • Under the Tudors, England increasingly moved towards the latter arrangement, emphasizing the importance of following laws created by Acts of Parliament.
Conflict with James I and Charles I:(pg 144)
...
  • Conflict arose after James I (r. 1603–25) and his successor Charles I sought to subordinate Parliament to the crown.
Outcome: (pg 144)
...
  • Parliamentarians won, and Charles I was executed, marking a decisive shift in the power balance between the monarchy and Parliament.

Civil war:
...

The English Civil Wars were an important turning point.
...
  • They brought abot a change in the ruling coalition.
  • Jha(2015) explores how emerging oversea opportunties help the formation of a coalition in favor of constraning the crown.
  • Elites purcahsed shares in overseas trading companies.
    • Aligning their interests in the merchant class.
  • Shifted the views of non-merchant and consolidate support for reformers in government.
Acceleration of Limited Goverance:
...
  • The civil wars significantly accelerated the ventual pre-eminence of limited governance.
Post-Civil War Dynamics
...
  • Despite the Parliamentarians emerging victorious, immediate implementation of limited governance did not occur.
  • The conflict between Parliament and the Stuart kings persisted, leading to another generation of conflict with James II.
Glorious Revolution
...
  • In 1688, a Parliamentary faction invited William of Orange and Mary to overthrow James II.
  • William and Mary came to the throne, culminating in the Glorious Revolution.
  • A Bill of Rights was accepted, relinquishing royal rights and limiting the power of the crown.

Foundation of party system (pg 145)
...

  • Glorious Revolution laid the foundations of the party system and cabinet government (Stasavage, 2002, 2003; Pincus and Robinson, 2014; Cox, 2016).
  • North and Weingast(1989):
    • Argued that the credibility of the monarchy post-1688 improved property rights.
    • The credibility translated into more secure property rights, particularly in terms of debt repayment.
Intitution quality:
...
  • There was an overall improvement in institutional quality after 1688.
  • The changes made it easier to rearrange property rights to exploit new investment opportunities.
Focus on Economic Opportunities:
...
  • The ability to rearrange residual claimancy in response to new economic opportunities became crucial.
  • The changes after 1688 allowed for the reordering of property rights to maximize productivity.
Evolution from Feudal to Modern Property Rights
...
  • While feudal property rights were secure, they were designed for a feudal society and not for maximizing productivity.
  • The transformations allowed for the evolution from complex and constrained 17th-century land rights to a more adaptable system.

Prior to the Glorious Revolution(pg 146-148)
...

  • Estate bills often failed due to political conflict.
  • Changes brought on by the Glorious Revolution led to reallocation of land towards more productive uses (Bogart and Richardson, 2009, 2011).

Consequences of Glorious Revolution
...

  • Investment in road and river transport improved dramatically.
  • Parliamentary regulation kept internal trade relatively unimpeded compared to other regions.
  • Parliament played a crucial role in regulating tolls on England’s road and canal network.
    • Tolls were kept low through Parliamentary regulation and market competition.
    • Toll roads competed with river and canal routes.

Political Dynamics
...

  • Limited democracy: Small proportion of adult males could vote.
  • After 1715, elections became less frequent (held only every seven years).
  • Power of the rich was entrenched, favoring the propertied classes.
  • Ruthless enforcement of Enclosure Acts and penal laws against the poor (Hay et al., 1975).

Changing Trends in Parliament
...

  • Early 18th-century Parliaments characterized by unabashed rent-seeking.
  • Over the 18th century, purely redistributional actions lost appeal (Mokyr and Nye, 2007).
  • Shift towards political stability and prosperity.
  • British system constrained independent power of the monarchy and balanced interests of landowning, mercantile, and financial elites.

Characteristics of British Political System
...

  • Not a democracy, limited political representation to those with property.
  • Scope for "voice" through regular elections.
  • Provided greater freedom for religious minorities.
  • Prohibited Catholics and religious dissenters from positions of political power (Johnson and Koyama, 2019).

Chapter Summary (pg 149-150)
...

  • By around 1700, northwestern Europe had preconditions for sustained economic growth.
  • Dutch Republic, though rich, did not experience sustained economic growth in the 18th century.
  • Dutch Republic followed a similar pattern to Italian city-states that grew rich on trade but eventually stagnated.
  • Britain experienced the first modern economy in the 18th and 19th centuries.

Factors Contributing to Dutch Stagnation
...

  • Rising inequality and entrenchment of political power among merchant elites.
  • Institutions like the Dutch East India Company (VOC) benefited a small number of shareholders.
  • High taxes, government debt, and mercantilist policies contributed to relative decline.

Differences Between Dutch Republic and Britain
...

  • Dutch Republic at the forefront of the Scientific Revolution.
  • Britain experienced a combination of industrial growth and structural change in the 18th and 19th centuries.
  • Britain's sustained economic growth was a result of institutional preconditions and additional factors.
